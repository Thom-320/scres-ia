# When Does Reinforcement Learning Work for Supply Chain Resilience? Action-Space Alignment in a Military DES Benchmark

## Abstract

Supply chain resilience research increasingly uses reinforcement learning and hybrid simulation workflows, but much of the literature still emphasizes inventory cost optimization, abstract network reconfiguration, or generic benchmark environments rather than operational control on validated, domain-specific models. We present a reproducible discrete-event simulation plus reinforcement learning benchmark for resilience control in a 13-operation military food supply chain rebuilt from Garrido-Rios' (2017) thesis and exposed through a Gymnasium-compatible interface. We first report a negative result: under the thesis-faithful control contract, PPO trained with a Cobb--Douglas resilience reward reaches fill rate 0.788 versus 0.792 for the strongest static baseline, and RecurrentPPO does not recover the gap. We then introduce a minimal action-space extension that adds downstream dispatch control at Op10 and Op12 while keeping the DES backbone intact. Under this extended contract, PPO reaches fill rate 1.000, zero backorders, and order-level resilience 0.950, exceeding the baseline by 3.40 percentage points in fill rate and the best static policy by 1.23 percentage points. A matched 5D-versus-7D ablation with identical observation space, reward family, and risk profile further indicates that the added downstream control dimensions are materially responsible for the gain. The contribution is not architectural novelty but a benchmark-centered diagnosis of when reinforcement learning fails and when it works in supply chain resilience control.

# 1. Introduction

Supply chain resilience (SCRES) has become a central concern in operations management as global disruptions---from pandemic-driven shutdowns to geopolitical trade barriers---expose the fragility of interconnected logistics networks. Over the past decade, reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for adaptive decision-making in supply chain management (SCM). Comprehensive reviews by Yan et al. (2022) and Rolf et al. (2023) document a growing body of work applying RL to inventory ordering, transportation scheduling, and multi-echelon coordination, while Boute et al. (2022) provide a design roadmap for deep RL in inventory control. More recently, Rolf et al. (2025) review 72 papers combining discrete-event simulation (DES) with machine learning, confirming that DES+RL is now the dominant hybrid paradigm for operational supply chain analysis. This paper therefore does not claim novelty in the idea of applying RL to supply chains; instead, it contributes a benchmark-centered investigation of *when* RL works and *when* it fails in resilience-oriented operational control.

Despite this maturation, most published RL-for-SCM work concentrates on inventory cost optimization in stylized or generic environments (Gijsbrechts et al. 2022; De Moor et al. 2022; Dehaybe et al. 2024; Geevers et al. 2024), while the few papers addressing resilience operate at the level of network reconfiguration rather than operational control. Ding et al. (2026) represent the closest resilience competitor: they use multi-agent PPO (MAPPO) for dynamic reconfiguration of a supply chain system-of-systems under disruption risks, with macro-level actions such as filling, repairing, and recruiting disrupted nodes. However, their problem is strategic topology redesign on an abstract network, fundamentally different from the weekly operational control---over assembly shifts, inventory quantities, and transport dispatch---that characterizes our setting. The gap addressed here is not whether RL can be applied to supply chains, but whether RL can deliver resilient operational control inside a thesis-grounded military supply chain whose structural bottlenecks are explicitly modeled.

This work builds directly on the research agenda of Garrido-Rios (2017) and Garrido et al. (2024a, 2024b). The PhD thesis developed a 13-operation military food supply chain (MFSC) simulation in MATLAB/Simulink, establishing quantitative resilience metrics (Re$^T$) through 90 static configurations and data-mining association rules. Garrido et al. (2024a) subsequently proposed a Cobb--Douglas factory resilience index linking production strategies to quantitative resilience measurement. Garrido et al. (2024b) explicitly called for the integration of AI algorithms---identifying neural networks, KANs, and reinforcement learning as the most suitable candidates---into simulation-based SCRES models, arguing that traditional DES frameworks lack endogenous learning mechanisms capable of adaptation across successive disruption events. The present study operationalizes this proposal: we rebuild the thesis DES in Python/SimPy, validate it against the original reference targets ($-4.43\%$ relative gap), wrap it in a Gymnasium-compatible interface, and apply PPO-based RL control.

Our core empirical finding is dual. Under Track A---the thesis-faithful control contract where the agent controls upstream inventory and assembly shifts (5 action dimensions)---PPO trained with a Cobb--Douglas resilience reward (ReT\_seq\_v1) reaches fill rate 0.788 versus 0.792 for the strongest static baseline (S=2), and RecurrentPPO does not recover the gap. No RL configuration across 14 reward modes, 5 observation versions, and 2 algorithms outperforms strong static policies. Under Track B---a minimal action-space extension that adds downstream transport dispatch at Op10 and Op12 (7 action dimensions) while preserving the DES backbone---PPO reaches fill rate 1.000, zero backorders, and order-level resilience 0.950, exceeding the S2 baseline by +3.40 percentage points in fill rate. A matched 5D-versus-7D ablation with identical observations, reward, and risk profile indicates that the downstream control dimensions are materially responsible for the gain. The paper's central argument is therefore structural: RL success in this benchmark depends not on algorithmic sophistication or reward engineering, but on whether the controllable interface reaches the binding operational bottleneck.

The paper makes four contributions. First, we report a negative empirical benchmark result (C1): under the thesis-faithful action contract, learned policies do not beat strong static baselines. Second, we report a positive empirical benchmark result (C2): a minimal action-space extension produces decisive RL advantage. Third, we provide a structural diagnosis supported by a matched ablation (C3): the downstream control dimensions are materially associated with the observed improvement inside this benchmark family. Fourth, we contribute a reproducible benchmark with auditable artifacts, frozen contracts, and a Cobb--Douglas resilience reward pipeline (C4). We do not claim to be the first paper on RL for supply chains, the first DES+RL study, or to offer architectural novelty; our contribution is experimental and diagnostic. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work across four blocks; Section 3 presents the methodology; Section 4 reports results; Section 5 discusses implications and limitations; and Section 6 concludes.

# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Reinforcement learning in logistics and supply chain management

Reinforcement learning has become an established paradigm for adaptive decision-making in supply chain management. Yan et al. (2022) provide a comprehensive review covering methodologies, state of the art, and opportunities for RL in logistics and SCM, documenting applications across inventory ordering, vehicle routing, and warehouse operations. Rolf et al. (2023) offer a semi-systematic review classifying RL algorithms and applications in SCM, noting that Q-learning variants have dominated historically but that policy-gradient methods such as PPO and A3C are gaining traction. Boute et al. (2022) establish a design roadmap for deep RL in inventory control, systematizing key choices around state representation, action formulation, and reward engineering. Powell (2022) provides the theoretical backdrop, arguing that RL's value-function approximation is only one of four policy classes and that simpler policy representations often outperform neural approaches in structured operations problems. These reviews confirm that RL in SCM is a mature field; our paper does not claim novelty in the *idea* of applying RL to supply chains but rather contributes a benchmark-centered diagnosis of when RL succeeds and when it does not.

## 2.2 Discrete-event simulation and machine learning for supply chains

The integration of discrete-event simulation with machine learning has accelerated sharply in recent years. Rolf et al. (2025) review 72 papers combining DES with ML for supply chain analysis, finding that 82% were published in the last five years and that RL is the most frequent ML paradigm in these hybrid pipelines. Perez-Pena et al. (2025) develop a classification framework applied to 99 simulation-ML papers, revealing that optimization is rarely incorporated into hybrid workflows. Our work sits squarely in this family: a custom SimPy DES wrapped with a Gymnasium interface for RL training. What distinguishes this benchmark from the typical DES+RL study is its grounding in a validated PhD thesis model (Garrido-Rios, 2017) rather than a synthetic or stylized environment, and its explicit treatment of control-contract design as the central experimental variable.

## 2.3 RL for operational and inventory control

The dominant application of RL in supply chains remains inventory and operational cost control. Gijsbrechts et al. (2022) apply A3C to lost-sales, dual-sourcing, and multi-echelon problems, achieving 3--6% optimality gaps but failing to consistently outperform capped base-stock policies. De Moor et al. (2022) demonstrate that potential-based reward shaping can stabilize DRL training for perishable inventory but does not always help the agent surpass teacher heuristics. Dehaybe et al. (2024) train PPO on non-stationary demand using rolling forecast horizons and achieve near-optimal lot-sizing policies. Geevers et al. (2024) apply PPO with continuous actions via Stable-Baselines3 to multi-echelon networks, reporting 6.6--16.4% cost reductions over classical benchmarks. Temizoz et al. (2025) propose Deep Controlled Learning, achieving optimality gaps below 0.2% and consistently outperforming all DRL baselines and heuristics. Vanvuchelen et al. (2025) address action-space scalability through continuous representations, finding that discrete formulations fail to scale to high-dimensional inventory problems.

These papers collectively show that the primary challenge for RL in inventory control is not architectural novelty but careful formulation of the MDP---state representation, reward design, and action-space structure. Our work extends this insight in a different direction: we show that even with a well-designed reward and a standard PPO algorithm, RL fails when the action space does not cover the binding operational constraint. The issue in Track A is not algorithmic weakness or reward misspecification but structural bottleneck misalignment.

## 2.4 Supply chain resilience, reconfiguration, and MARL

The closest competitor in the resilience literature is Ding et al. (2026), who apply MAPPO to dynamic reconfiguration of a supply chain system-of-systems under disruption risks, with macro-level actions (filling, repairing, recruiting) operating on an abstract network topology. Their contribution is strategic topology redesign; ours is operational control within a fixed, detailed DES. The distinction is not cosmetic: their state space represents network-level attributes (node density, clustering coefficients), while ours represents operational variables (inventory levels, disruption flags, shift configuration) at hourly granularity. Rolf et al. (2024) study supply chain recovery under disruptions using system dynamics coupled with RL, demonstrating that RL can learn robust order policies without historical data. Kim (2024) proposes cooperative MARL for inventory transshipment during disruptions. Liu et al. (2025) apply heterogeneous-agent PPO (HAPPO) to decentralized multi-echelon inventory, finding that hybrid rewards outperform purely self-interested or cooperative objectives. Kotecha and del Rio Chanona (2025) combine graph neural networks with MAPPO for inventory control in supply chain networks.

These MARL and resilience papers address coordination across organizational boundaries or topology reconfiguration. Our single-agent approach asks a simpler but complementary question: given a fixed supply chain topology, does expanding the agent's operational control scope to include the active bottleneck change the benchmark outcome? The Track B result suggests that when the bottleneck can be identified and controlled by a single agent, centralized control with appropriate action scope suffices.

## 2.5 Positioning summary

This paper does not claim to be the first RL-for-SCM or DES+RL study. Its contribution is a thesis-grounded benchmark and structural diagnosis showing that RL success in resilience control depends on action-space alignment with the active operational constraint---a finding that connects bottleneck reasoning from the Theory of Constraints (Goldratt) with RL agent design in a way that, to our knowledge, has not been demonstrated empirically in the published literature.

# 3. Methodology


## 3.1 MFSC discrete-event simulation

The experimental platform is a 13-operation military food supply chain reconstructed in Python/SimPy from Garrido-Rios' thesis. The model preserves the original operational structure rather than replacing it with a stylized benchmark environment. This matters for the paper's positioning: the contribution is not a generic RL sandbox, but a thesis-grounded operational model whose buffers, transport stages, and disruption processes already carry domain meaning.

The DES retains hourly granularity where the original supply chain requires sub-day behavior, especially around the assembly-line operations. This avoids collapsing operational effects that would disappear under daily aggregation, including short breakdowns and shift-sensitive capacity changes. The warm-up regime also follows the validated thesis lineage, with the first full batch reaching the downstream chain after roughly `838.8` simulated hours. The RL layer therefore sits on top of a model that is already operationally constrained before learning begins.

The simulation backbone is not treated as a moving target. Deterministic and stochastic checks remain anchored to the thesis reference tables, and the benchmark story is built on the audited post-alignment DES rather than on earlier historical artifacts. This allows the paper to claim benchmark fidelity without claiming that the model is a universal representation of all supply chains.

## 3.2 Control formulation

The learning problem is defined as weekly operational control over the DES. At each decision epoch, the agent observes a state vector derived from the simulation, selects a continuous action, and receives a resilience-oriented reward. Decisions are taken every `168` simulated hours, which matches the weekly planning cadence already embedded in procurement, disruption, and operating rhythms. For manuscript language, the problem should be described as an augmented observed-state control problem, or equivalently a practical `POMDP`, rather than as a fully sufficient MDP.

The paper uses two control contracts. `Track A` is the thesis-faithful benchmark family. It uses observation version `v1` with `15` dimensions and a `5D` action contract covering upstream quantity and reorder multipliers together with assembly-shift selection. This is the negative paper lane: it asks whether RL can beat strong static policies when control remains limited to the original scope.

`Track B` is a separate research lane rather than a retrofit of `Track A`. It uses observation version `v7` with `46` dimensions and a `7D` action contract. The first five actions keep the Track A controls, while the sixth and seventh actions add downstream dispatch multipliers at `Op10` and `Op12`. The paper's central question is whether this minimal extension changes the benchmark outcome by exposing the operational bottleneck that Track A leaves mostly outside the controllable interface.

## 3.3 Reward and reporting metrics

The main paper lane uses `ReT_seq_v1` with `ret_seq_kappa = 0.20` as the training reward. The reason for centering this reward is not that it is the only reward ever tested in the repository, but that it is the best-aligned resilience-index family that remains smooth enough for policy-gradient learning while still preserving a visible link to the thesis resilience framing. In the current paper story, reward design and control-contract design are discussed jointly: Track A shows that a reasonable resilience-aware reward is not enough when the controllable action scope misses the binding downstream constraint, whereas Track B shows that the same reward can support strong learning once the action contract is repaired.

The paper-facing comparison should therefore prioritize service and resilience outcomes rather than raw reward totals across incompatible families. The primary metrics for the main claim are `fill_rate`, `backorder_rate`, `order_level_ret_mean`, and `terminal_rolling_fill_rate_4w`. Secondary audit metrics such as `ret_thesis_corrected_total`, `ret_unified_total`, and the order-case shares remain useful for interpretation, but they should not replace the main service/resilience metrics when the paper compares policies across benchmark families.

One language rule should stay explicit in the final manuscript: `reward_total` is only comparable within a shared reward family. Whenever the paper compares Track A and Track B, or different reward families inside Track A, the argument should rely on service and resilience metrics rather than on raw cumulative reward.

## 3.4 Experimental protocol

The experimental design contains two benchmark families and one matched ablation. `Track A` is the thesis-faithful benchmark lane. It retains the validated DES backbone, the original operational scope, and strong static baselines as the main comparators. Learned policies are reported honestly as negative evidence when they remain below the strongest static baseline; this includes PPO in the frozen `ReT_seq_v1` lane and RecurrentPPO in the corresponding recurrent comparator lane.

`Track B` is the positive paper lane. Its frozen contract is `env_variant="track_b_adaptive_control"`, `reward_mode="ReT_seq_v1"`, `ret_seq_kappa=0.20`, `observation_version="v7"`, `action_contract="track_b_v1"`, `risk_level="adaptive_benchmark_v2"`, `year_basis="thesis"`, `step_size_hours=168`, and `stochastic_pt=True`. The production benchmark is trained for `500000` timesteps with seeds `11 22 33 44 55` and evaluated over `20` episodes per seed. Static comparators are `s2_d1.00`, `s3_d1.00`, and `s3_d2.00`.

To separate the action-contract argument from broader environment changes, the paper also uses a matched `5D vs 7D` ablation. Within that ablation family, the observation version, reward family, risk profile, and training budget are held fixed while only the action contract changes. This is the cleanest evidence available inside the current repo for the claim that downstream control coverage is materially responsible for the Track B gain. The manuscript should still state the limitation clearly: the ablation is supportive causal evidence inside the frozen benchmark family, not universal proof outside it.

# 4. Results

## 4.1 DES validation

The reconstructed DES was first validated against the deterministic baseline (Cf0, single shift S=1) reported in Garrido-Rios (2017, Table 6.10). Using the official thesis basis of 8,064 hours per year (48 operational weeks), the simulation delivers 733,621 rations per year on average, compared with the thesis reference of 767,592 rations, yielding a relative gap of $-4.43\%$. This is comfortably within the accepted $\pm 15\%$ validation threshold and supports the structural fidelity of the rebuilt DES. A secondary Gregorian-basis check (8,760 hours per year) produces 796,940 rations per year ($+3.82\%$ relative gap), confirming that the discrepancy is driven by year-basis conventions rather than model error (Table 4).

Under deterministic conditions with risks disabled, the simulation produces 734,458 rations per year and delivers 733,621, with a fill rate of 99.3% and only 41 backorders accumulated over the 20-year horizon. When stochastic risks are enabled at the current level, average annual delivery falls to 677,750 rations ($-7.6\%$ relative to the deterministic baseline), the fill rate drops to 68.3%, and total backorders rise to 1,825. The model registers 8,247 disruptive events across the 20-year horizon, mainly driven by internal workstation breakdowns (R11: 1,868 events), defective products (R14: 5,591 events), and recurrent demand surges (R24: 478 events). Under the increased risk configuration used for the benchmark, average annual delivery decreases further to 549,250 rations, fill rate falls to 45.6%, and total backorders increase to 3,132. The DES therefore shows a clear transition from a well-calibrated nominal system to a fragile network once disruption intensity escalates.

## 4.2 Track A: thesis-faithful benchmark (negative result)

Under the thesis-faithful Track A contract, no RL configuration outperforms the strongest static baseline. Table 8 summarizes the primary results. PPO trained with ReT\_seq\_v1 ($\kappa = 0.20$) over 500,000 timesteps across 5--10 seeds reaches a cumulative fill rate of 0.788, falling short of static S2 at 0.792 by 0.4 percentage points. PPO trained with the linear control\_v1 reward achieves fill rate 0.782, losing by 1.0 percentage point. RecurrentPPO (LSTM-based) trained with control\_v1 performs worse still at fill rate 0.751, losing by 4.1 percentage points. A tuned heuristic baseline reaches 0.795, approximately matching S2 but not materially exceeding it.

The negative result is not an algorithmic failure in the conventional sense. A design-of-experiments analysis reveals that the Track A action space exhibits strongly asymmetric sensitivity: destructive inventory settings reduce reward by 44--65% and can collapse fill rate, while the positive headroom above S2-neutral inventory control is limited to approximately 1% in reward. PPO's convergence to near-neutral action values on the inventory dimensions, combined with S2-region shift selection, is therefore a rational response to asymmetric downside risk. The structural explanation is the downstream distribution bottleneck identified in finding F11: Op10--Op12 transport throughput ($\approx$2,500 rations/day) approximately equals demand, so extra upstream production capacity from S3 yields sharply diminishing returns. S2 already saturates the downstream pipeline; S3 produces more but delivers approximately the same. Because Track A does not expose downstream dispatch control to the agent, the active constraint lies outside the controllable interface.

## 4.3 Track B: extended action space (positive result)

Track B extends the action space from 5 to 7 dimensions by adding downstream dispatch multipliers at Op10 and Op12, while preserving the DES backbone and reward family. Table 9 presents the Track B results from the production benchmark (500,000 timesteps, 5 seeds $\times$ 20 evaluation episodes).

PPO achieves fill rate 1.000 (zero backorders), cumulative reward 254.21, and order-level resilience (ReT mean) 0.950. The S2 baseline with neutral dispatch (s2\_d1.00) reaches fill rate 0.966 and reward 178.24; the best static policy (S3 with double dispatch, s3\_d2.00) reaches fill rate 0.988 and reward 171.06. PPO therefore exceeds s2\_d1.00 by +3.40 percentage points in fill rate and the best static policy by +1.23 percentage points. The reward gap versus the best static policy is +83.16, reflecting both service improvement and cost efficiency.

The learned policy's shift utilization is 78% S1 / 16% S2 / 6% S3---a marked contrast to static S2 (100% S2) and static S3 (100% S3). This indicates that PPO learned to operate at the lowest assembly cost (S1) for most of the episode while activating higher shifts selectively when downstream conditions require surge capacity. The combination of full fill rate with predominantly S1 operation represents a cost-efficient strategy that no static policy achieves: static S3+d2.00 reaches near-perfect fill but at permanently elevated assembly cost, while PPO matches or exceeds its fill rate at substantially lower average shift utilization.

## 4.4 Matched ablation: 5D versus 7D

To isolate the effect of downstream control from other differences between Track A and Track B (observation dimensionality, risk profile), we conduct a matched ablation holding all factors constant except the action contract. Both conditions use observation version v7 (46 dimensions), reward ReT\_seq\_v1 ($\kappa = 0.20$), risk level adaptive\_benchmark\_v2, and a training budget of 100,000 timesteps across 3 seeds. The only difference is the action space: 5D (Track A actions) versus 7D (Track A actions + Op10/Op12 dispatch).

Table 10 reports the results. Under the 5D condition, PPO reaches fill rate 0.607, losing to static S2 (fill rate 0.616) by 0.9 percentage points. Under the 7D condition, PPO reaches fill rate 0.964, winning by +34.8 percentage points over the same S2 baseline. The fill-rate difference between conditions is +35.7 percentage points. The reward difference is +73.44.

Because the observation space, reward function, risk profile, and training budget are identical across conditions, this matched comparison provides strong evidence that the downstream control dimensions---not the enriched observation space or the adaptive risk profile alone---are materially responsible for the observed Track B gain. The manuscript adopts careful language: this ablation *indicates* and *supports the interpretation* that action-space alignment with the active bottleneck determines RL effectiveness in this benchmark. We do not claim universal causal proof outside the frozen benchmark family, and we note that the ablation uses a shorter training horizon (100k versus 500k) and fewer seeds (3 versus 5) than the production Track B run.

## 4.5 Summary

Table 11 provides a unified comparison across all conditions. The experimental evidence forms a coherent narrative: Track A demonstrates a structural limitation, Track B demonstrates that a minimal action-space repair overcomes it, and the matched ablation localizes the effect to the downstream control dimensions. The contribution is not that RL is universally effective for supply chain resilience, but that its effectiveness depends critically on whether the controllable interface reaches the binding operational constraint.

# 5. Discussion

## 5.1 Interpreting the Track A negative result

The Track A result should be read as evidence of a structural limitation rather than as evidence that RL is ineffective for supply chains in general. Under the thesis-faithful contract, the static S2 policy already operates near the practical ceiling imposed by downstream distribution capacity: Op10--Op12 transport throughput of approximately 2,500 rations per day roughly matches demand, so additional upstream production capacity from S3 generates excess inventory in intermediate buffers without proportionally increasing deliveries. The design-of-experiments analysis confirms an asymmetric action landscape in which destructive inventory settings reduce reward by 44--65% while positive headroom above S2-neutral is limited to approximately 1%. In this regime, PPO's convergence to near-neutral inventory actions with S2-region shift selection is rational risk management rather than learning failure. Reward redesign (14 modes tested), memory (RecurrentPPO), and richer observations (v2--v6) do not overturn this structural limitation because the binding constraint lies outside the controllable interface.

## 5.2 What Track B changes

Track B changes the controllable interface, not the underlying DES physics. The positive result therefore supports a diagnosis about control scope rather than a story about algorithmic sophistication. By adding Op10 and Op12 dispatch multipliers to the action space, the agent gains leverage over the downstream bottleneck that constrains Track A. The learned policy exploits this by running predominantly at S1 (78% of steps)---the lowest-cost assembly configuration---while modulating downstream dispatch to maintain perfect fill. This shift mix is a strategy that no static policy can replicate: static S3+d2.00 achieves near-perfect fill but at permanently elevated assembly cost, while PPO matches or exceeds it at substantially lower average capacity utilization. The cost-efficiency implication is that the agent learned not just *what* to deliver but *how cheaply* to deliver it when downstream flows are controllable.

## 5.3 Action-space alignment as a design principle

The dual-track result connects three literatures that have not previously been linked empirically: the Theory of Constraints (Goldratt), which identifies the binding constraint as the lever that determines system throughput; RL agent design, which determines what the agent can control; and action-space architecture, which determines how decisions are represented. Our benchmark demonstrates a concrete instance of this connection: when the action space does not cover the active constraint (Track A), better algorithms and rewards cannot compensate; when it does (Track B), a standard PPO+MLP agent produces decisive advantage. This suggests a practical design principle for RL-for-SCRES applications: before investing in architectural novelty or reward engineering, verify that the agent's controllable interface reaches the binding operational bottleneck.

## 5.4 Limitations

Several limitations bound the generalizability of these findings. First, the matched ablation uses 100,000 training timesteps and 3 seeds, a shorter budget than the production Track B benchmark (500,000 timesteps, 5 seeds). While the effect size is large (+35.7 percentage points in fill rate), stronger replication would increase confidence. Second, the Track B risk profile (adaptive\_benchmark\_v2) differs from Track A's (increased), so the production comparison across tracks conflates action-contract changes with risk-profile changes. The matched ablation addresses this by holding the risk profile constant, but the 100k budget remains a limitation. Third, the problem is better described as partially observed control than a fully identified MDP: the agent observes a projection of the simulation state, not the full internal state. Fourth, the benchmark is one validated military supply chain, not a universal SCRES environment; generalization to other topologies, demand patterns, or organizational structures requires further investigation. Finally, ReT\_seq\_v1 is a training reward designed for gradient quality, not a theoretically complete resilience index; the paper-facing reporting therefore uses multiple complementary metrics rather than a single reward-based ranking.

## 5.5 Future work

Three directions emerge from this benchmark. First, the ablation should be scaled to production-grade replication (500k timesteps, 10+ seeds) with formal statistical testing to strengthen the causal interpretation. Second, the action-space alignment principle should be tested on other validated supply chain DES models to assess whether the finding generalizes beyond the MFSC topology. Third, more expressive policy architectures---including attention-based networks, graph neural networks, and emerging KAN-based designs---remain relevant for supply chains with variable topology or multi-agent coordination, but they were deliberately excluded from this paper because the standard PPO+MLP architecture already achieves fill rate 1.000 under Track B. Architectural extension is future work, not a requirement of the present benchmark.

# 6. Conclusion

This paper presented a reproducible DES+RL benchmark for operational resilience control in a military food supply chain, grounded in Garrido-Rios' (2017) validated 13-operation simulation model. The experimental design uses two matched benchmark families to answer a structural question: when does reinforcement learning outperform strong static baselines in resilience-oriented operational control?

Under the thesis-faithful Track A contract, where the agent controls upstream inventory and assembly shifts (5 action dimensions), no RL configuration across 14 reward modes, 5 observation versions, and 2 algorithms outperforms the strongest static baseline. The root cause is structural: the active downstream distribution bottleneck lies outside the agent's controllable interface, limiting the marginal value of upstream optimization regardless of algorithmic sophistication.

Under the extended Track B contract, which adds downstream transport dispatch at Op10 and Op12 (7 action dimensions) while preserving the DES backbone, PPO achieves fill rate 1.000, zero backorders, and order-level resilience 0.950, decisively exceeding all static baselines. A matched 5D-versus-7D ablation with identical observations, reward, and risk profile indicates that the downstream control dimensions are materially responsible for the observed gain.

The central lesson is that RL effectiveness in this benchmark depends not on algorithmic novelty or reward engineering but on whether the controllable interface reaches the binding operational constraint. This finding connects bottleneck reasoning from the Theory of Constraints with RL action-space design---a connection that, to our knowledge, has not been demonstrated empirically in the supply chain literature. For practitioners and researchers designing RL-based supply chain control systems, the implication is direct: before investing in architectural complexity, verify that the agent can act on the constraint that actually limits system performance.
