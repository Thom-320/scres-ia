\section{Methodology}\label{3-methodology}}

\hypertarget{31-mfsc-discrete-event-simulation}{%
\subsection{MFSC discrete-event
simulation}\label{31-mfsc-discrete-event-simulation}}

The experimental platform is a 13-operation military food supply chain
reconstructed in Python/SimPy from Garrido-Rios' thesis~\cite{garrido2017}
and checked against the original Garrido workbook artifacts used for
raw order ledgers and APj/RPj/DPj/Re summaries. The model preserves the
original operational structure rather than replacing it with a stylized
benchmark environment. This matters for the paper's positioning: the
contribution is not a generic RL sandbox, but a thesis-grounded
operational model whose buffers, transport stages, and disruption
processes already carry domain meaning.

The DES retains hourly granularity where the original supply chain
requires sub-day behavior, especially around the assembly-line
operations. This avoids collapsing operational effects that would
disappear under daily aggregation, including short breakdowns and
shift-sensitive capacity changes. The warm-up regime also follows the
validated thesis lineage, with the first full batch reaching the
downstream chain after roughly \texttt{838.8} simulated hours. The RL
layer therefore sits on top of a model that is already operationally
constrained before learning begins.

The simulation backbone is not treated as a moving target. Deterministic
and stochastic checks remain anchored to the thesis reference tables,
and the benchmark story is built on the audited post-alignment DES
rather than on earlier historical artifacts. This allows the paper to
claim benchmark fidelity without claiming that the model is a universal
representation of all supply chains.

The stochastic risk environment preserves the full thesis risk
architecture: four operational known-known risks (R11--R14), four
known-unknown risks (R21--R24), and one black-swan event (R3), with the
thesis probability models summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:risk_provenance_examples}. Regular demand is discrete
uniform $U(2{,}400,\,2{,}600)$ rations per day, six days per week. The
benchmark risk regimes used later in the paper (\texttt{current},
\texttt{increased}, \texttt{severe}, and the sustained-disruption
\texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2} cell) scale the frequency parameters
of these same nine processes rather than introducing new disruption
mechanisms, so every stress cell remains inside the thesis risk
architecture. The table is not an additional result; it documents that
the benchmark preserves the stochastic disruption semantics that make
the MFSC a Garrido-grounded DES rather than a generic
inventory-control toy problem.

\hypertarget{32-control-formulation}{%
\subsection{Control formulation}\label{32-control-formulation}}

The learning problem is defined as weekly operational control over the
DES. At each decision epoch, the agent observes a state vector derived
from the simulation, selects a continuous action, and receives a
resilience-oriented reward. Decisions are taken every \texttt{168}
simulated hours, which matches the weekly planning cadence already
embedded in procurement, disruption, and operating rhythms. Formally, we
treat the simulator-control loop as a finite-horizon partially observed
control problem
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{M}=(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{O},\mathcal{A},P,r,\gamma,H),
\label{eq:pomdp}
\end{equation}
where $s_t\in\mathcal{S}$ is the full DES state (queues, buffers,
orders, disruptions, and resource states), $o_t\in\mathcal{O}$ is the
feature vector exposed to the policy, $a_t\in\mathcal{A}$ is the
weekly operational-control action, $P$ is the simulator transition
kernel induced by SimPy events and stochastic disruptions, $r$ is the
training reward, $\gamma$ is the discount factor, and $H$ is the
evaluation horizon (52 or 104 weeks in the generalization study). The
paper-facing outcome is not raw training reward but a trajectory-level
metric panel $G(\tau)$ headed by Garrido/Excel ReT.

The paper uses two control contracts. \texttt{Track\ A} is the
thesis-grounded benchmark family. It uses observation version
\texttt{v1} with \texttt{15} dimensions and a \texttt{5D} action
contract covering upstream quantity and reorder multipliers together
with assembly-shift selection. This is the negative paper lane: it asks
whether RL can beat strong static policies when control remains limited
to the original scope.

\texttt{Track\ B} is a separate research lane rather than a retrofit of
\texttt{Track\ A}. It uses observation version \texttt{v7} with
\texttt{52} dimensions and the \texttt{track\_b\_v1} action contract
(\texttt{8D}). The action contract keeps the Track A upstream
replenishment and shift controls, adds an Op5 pre-assembly quantity
multiplier, and --- decisively --- adds
downstream dispatch multipliers at \texttt{Op10} and \texttt{Op12};
Table~\ref{tab:action_contract} gives the exact decode. The
paper's central question is whether this operational extension changes
the benchmark outcome by exposing the operational bottleneck that Track
A leaves mostly outside the controllable interface.

Operationally, the Op10/Op12 dispatch multipliers represent short-term
downstream transport expediting authority --- additional dispatch
frequency, vehicle allocation, or priority release at the two
line-of-communication stages feeding the combat service support units and
the theatre of operations. They do not alter demand, risk realization, or
upstream production physics; Track B preserves the DES process logic and
risk structure while extending the controllable decision interface.
Because the Garrido-Rios source does not provide calibrated transport
cost data, dispatch costs are treated through an explicit sensitivity
analysis (Section~4.3) rather than as calibrated monetary costs.

\hypertarget{33-reward-and-reporting-metrics}{%
\subsection{Reward and reporting
metrics}\label{33-reward-and-reporting-metrics}}

The canonical Track B training reward is \texttt{control\_v1}, a sparse
operational penalty rather than a resilience-index reward. For weekly
decision step $t$,
\begin{equation}
r_t =
-\left[
w_{\mathrm{bo}}\frac{BO_t}{\max(D_t,1)}
+ w_{\mathrm{shift}}(S_t-1)
+ w_{\mathrm{disr}}
\min\left(1,\frac{H_t}{168|\mathcal{K}|}\right)
\right],
\label{eq:control_v1}
\end{equation}
where $BO_t$ is new backordered quantity, $D_t$ is new demanded quantity,
$S_t\in\{1,2,3\}$ is the selected assembly-shift level, $H_t$ is
step-level disruption time over the tracked operations $\mathcal{K}$, and
the canonical Track B weights are $w_{\mathrm{bo}}=1.0$,
$w_{\mathrm{shift}}=0.06$, and $w_{\mathrm{disr}}=0.0$. The inactive
disruption term is retained in the equation because it is part of the
frozen environment contract, but it does not contribute under the
canonical run. This reward is a deliberate design choice, not an
oversight: the agent is trained on an operational proxy and evaluated on
the untouched Garrido/Excel resilience metric, so the training signal
cannot be tuned to directly game the reported evaluation metric. The
Cobb--Douglas-style reward equation in Garrido's draft is therefore
historical context for the broader AI-SCRES agenda, not the training
objective for the winning Track B policy. Track A results draw on a
broader set of resilience-aligned reward families (including
Cobb--Douglas and sequential formulations) tested across the
dense-frontier evaluation; no tested reward family closes the Track A
gap.

The primary reported metric follows the original Garrido workbook branch
used in the order ledgers, which operationalizes the thesis measure
(Garrido-Rios 2017, Eq.~5.5~\cite{garrido2017}): order states are
weighted at $\overline{\mathrm{Re}}^{\max}{=}1$ during autonomy,
$\overline{\mathrm{Re}}{=}0.5$ during recovery, and
$\overline{\mathrm{Re}}^{\min}{=}0$ during non-recovery. For order $j$,
let $OPT_j$ and $OAT_j$ be its placement and fulfillment times,
$CT_j = OAT_j - OPT_j$ the realized cycle time, $LT_j = 48$~h the
promised lead time, $\bar{R}_j$ the mean of the recorded risk
indicators on the order window, $AP_j$ the
autonomy period (risk-exposure time absorbed while still delivering on
time), $RP_j$ the recovery period when the order is served late, and
$B_j, U_j$ the running counts of backordered and unattended orders. The
Excel resilience score is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{ReT}^{\mathrm{Excel}}_j =
\begin{cases}
AP_j/LT_j, & \bar{R}_j>0,\ AP_j>0,\\[2mm]
0.5\,(1/RP_j), & \bar{R}_j>0,\ AP_j=0,\ RP_j>0,\\[2mm]
0, & \bar{R}_j>0,\ AP_j{=}RP_j{=}0,\ \text{or undelivered},\\[2mm]
1-\bigl(\min(B_j,60)+U_j\bigr)/j, & \bar{R}_j=0,
\end{cases}
\label{eq:ret}
\end{equation}
the LaTeX rendering of the workbook formula in the original
\texttt{Raw\_data+Re} sheets
(\texttt{IF(AVERAGE(R..)>0, IF(APj>0, APj/LT, 0.5*(1/RPj)), 1-((Bt+Ut)/j)})),
including the workbook's backorder-count cap of 60 in the fill-rate
branch. Our implementation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ret} is audited row-by-row
against Garrido's original result workbooks (47{,}546 orders, zero
mismatches). \emph{Order-level ReT} applies the same case structure with
the DES's native risk attribution instead of the workbook-visible
indicators; both conventions are reported.
Figure~\ref{fig:ret_branch_timeline} illustrates the branch logic on
three representative orders. Eq.~\eqref{eq:ret} also grounds the ceiling
interpretation used in Section~4: in the canonical stress regime 99.6\%
of orders fall in the recovery branch, and no evaluated policy realizes
a recovery period below 72~h (the 48~h promised lead time plus one 24~h
downstream dispatch cycle), so the per-order score is empirically capped
at $0.5/72 \approx 0.006944$.

The paper-facing comparison prioritizes service and resilience outcomes
rather than raw reward totals across incompatible reward families. The
primary metric for the headline claim is \texttt{order\_ret\_excel}
(Garrido/Excel ReT), with \texttt{order\_level\_ret\_mean},
\texttt{flow\_fill\_rate}, \texttt{terminal\_rolling\_fill\_rate\_4w},
recovery-tail metrics (\texttt{order\_ctj\_p99},
\texttt{order\_rpj\_p99}), service-loss AUC, and a shift-utilization cost
index (an assembly-labor proxy; it does not price dispatch, which is
costed separately through the sensitivity in Section~4.3)
reported as a secondary evidence panel, not substitutes for the primary
metric. \texttt{reward\_total} is only comparable within a shared reward
family; comparisons across Track A and Track B, or across reward
families within Track A, rely on the evaluation metric panel rather than
on raw cumulative reward.

\hypertarget{34-experimental-protocol}{%
\subsection{Experimental protocol}\label{34-experimental-protocol}}

The experimental design contains two benchmark families and one matched
ablation. \texttt{Track\ A} is the thesis-grounded benchmark lane. It
retains the validated DES backbone, the original operational scope, and
strong static baselines as the main comparators. Learned policies are
reported honestly as negative evidence when they remain below the
strongest static baseline; this includes PPO in the frozen
\texttt{ReT\_seq\_v1} lane and RecurrentPPO in the corresponding
recurrent comparator lane.

\texttt{Track\ B} is the positive paper lane. Its canonical contract fixes
the reward (\texttt{control\_v1}), the observation version (\texttt{v7},
52 dimensions), the action contract (\texttt{track\_b\_v1}, 8 dimensions),
the risk level (\texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2}), the thesis year basis,
a 168-hour decision step, and stochastic processing times. The confirmatory benchmark is trained for
\texttt{60{,}000} timesteps per seed with seeds \texttt{1--5} and
evaluated over \texttt{12} common-random-number episodes per seed against
a dense downstream-dispatch static grid of 147 comparators (3 shift
levels $\times$
7 Op10 multipliers $\times$ 7 Op12 multipliers, each in $\{0.5, 0.75,
1.0, 1.25, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5\}$). The grid is dense over the dispatch/shift
family that Track B is designed to test; the remaining upstream
buffer/reorder dimensions are held at their canonical Track B settings
for all static comparators, and learned control over that upstream family
alone is separately bounded by the Track A dense-frontier negative result
(Section~4.2). As a local static-comparator bound, we also evaluate a
3$\times$3 grid over Op3 and Op9 quantity multipliers
(\{0.75, 1.00, 1.25\}) at the best downstream-dispatch cell
(S2, Op10$\times$2.00, Op12$\times$1.50) under the same CRN protocol.
This bound is not an exhaustive eight-dimensional frontier, but it tests
whether the nearest upstream static degrees of freedom overturn the
downstream-grid result.
The learner is PPO with a two-hidden-layer
(64$\times$64) multilayer-perceptron policy and value network
(\texttt{MlpPolicy}, separate actor/critic heads), learning rate
$3\times10^{-4}$, $n_{\mathrm{steps}}{=}1024$, batch size 256, 10 epochs
per update, $\gamma{=}0.99$, GAE parameter $0.95$, and clip range 0.2 (the symbol $\lambda_d$ is reserved for the dispatch cost charge of Section~4.3).

\subsection{Statistical inference}\label{35-statistical-inference}

All learned-versus-static comparisons are paired under common random
numbers~\cite{law2015simulation}: every policy is evaluated on identical
disruption and demand realizations, so policy differences are isolated
from scenario noise. The promoted Track B headline uses 120 evaluation
pairs (10 seeds $\times$ 12 episodes); the E1-E4 mechanism checks and
top-12 appendix use their original 5-seed paired designs. Episode-level
pooled confidence intervals are reported as descriptive only. Primary
inference treats the \emph{training seed} as the inferential unit:
per-seed mean paired deltas are computed first, and claims rest on seed
sign consistency together with seed-clustered bootstrap or $t$-based
intervals. Where tail risk is reported, we use the lower conditional
tail of the episode distribution,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CVaR}_{0.05}(X) \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_{(i)},
\qquad n = \max\bigl(1, \lceil 0.05\,N \rceil\bigr),
\label{eq:cvar}
\end{equation}
the mean of the $n$ smallest per-episode values $X_{(1)} \le \dots \le
X_{(N)}$ of the metric. To guard against winner's-curse comparator
selection, the headline delta is additionally recomputed against each of
the twelve strongest static comparators individually; all twelve deltas
remain positive with confidence intervals excluding zero
(Appendix~\ref{appendix-robustness}). Secondary metrics (fill, tails,
cost) are reported as an evidence panel without multiplicity adjustment
and are not used as stand-alone claims.

To separate the action-contract argument from action-space size, the
paper uses a matched action-space ablation at the same 5-seed,
60{,}000-timestep scale, comparing the full \texttt{joint} 8D contract
against \texttt{downstream\_only} (shift frozen) and \texttt{shift\_only}
(dispatch frozen) variants, with observation version, reward family,
risk profile, and training budget held fixed. This decomposition provides
mechanism evidence that downstream control access, not action-space
size, drives the Track B gain; it is supportive evidence inside the
studied benchmark family, not causal proof outside
it.
