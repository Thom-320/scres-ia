\hypertarget{41-des-validation}{%
\subsection{4.1 DES validation}\label{41-des-validation}}

The reconstructed DES was first validated against the deterministic
baseline (Cf0, single shift S=1) reported in Garrido-Rios (2017, Table
6.10). Using the official thesis basis of 8,064 hours per year (48
operational weeks), the simulation delivers 733,621 rations per year on
average, compared with the thesis reference of 767,592 rations, yielding
a relative gap of \$-4.43\%\$. This is comfortably within the accepted
\$\textbackslash pm 15\%\$ validation threshold and supports the
structural fidelity of the rebuilt DES. A secondary Gregorian-basis
check (8,760 hours per year) produces 796,940 rations per year
(\$+3.82\%\$ relative gap), confirming that the discrepancy is driven by
year-basis conventions rather than model error (Table 4).

Under deterministic conditions with risks disabled, the simulation
produces 734,458 rations per year and delivers 733,621, with a fill rate
of 99.3\% and only 41 backorders accumulated over the 20-year horizon.
When stochastic risks are enabled at the current level, average annual
delivery falls to 677,750 rations (\$-7.6\%\$ relative to the
deterministic baseline), the fill rate drops to 68.3\%, and total
backorders rise to 1,825. The model registers 8,247 disruptive events
across the 20-year horizon, mainly driven by internal workstation
breakdowns (R11: 1,868 events), defective products (R14: 5,591 events),
and recurrent demand surges (R24: 478 events). Under the increased risk
configuration used for the benchmark, average annual delivery decreases
further to 549,250 rations, fill rate falls to 45.6\%, and total
backorders increase to 3,132. The DES therefore shows a clear transition
from a well-calibrated nominal system to a fragile network once
disruption intensity escalates.

\hypertarget{42-track-a-thesis-faithful-benchmark-negative-result}{%
\subsection{4.2 Track A: thesis-faithful benchmark (negative
result)}\label{42-track-a-thesis-faithful-benchmark-negative-result}}

Under the thesis-faithful Track A contract, no RL configuration
outperforms the strongest static baseline. Table 8 summarizes the
primary results. PPO trained with ReT\_seq\_v1 (\$\textbackslash kappa =
0.20\$) over 500,000 timesteps across 5-\/-10 seeds reaches a cumulative
fill rate of 0.788, falling short of static S2 at 0.792 by 0.4
percentage points. PPO trained with the linear control\_v1 reward
achieves fill rate 0.782, losing by 1.0 percentage point. RecurrentPPO
(LSTM-based) trained with control\_v1 performs worse still at fill rate
0.751, losing by 4.1 percentage points. A tuned heuristic baseline
reaches 0.795, approximately matching S2 but not materially exceeding
it.

The negative result is not an algorithmic failure in the conventional
sense. A design-of-experiments analysis reveals that the Track A action
space exhibits strongly asymmetric sensitivity: destructive inventory
settings reduce reward by 44-\/-65\% and can collapse fill rate, while
the positive headroom above S2-neutral inventory control is limited to
approximately 1\% in reward. PPO's convergence to near-neutral action
values on the inventory dimensions, combined with S2-region shift
selection, is therefore a rational response to asymmetric downside risk.
The structural explanation is the downstream distribution bottleneck
identified in finding F11: Op10-\/-Op12 transport throughput
(\$\textbackslash approx\$2,500 rations/day) approximately equals
demand, so extra upstream production capacity from S3 yields sharply
diminishing returns. S2 already saturates the downstream pipeline; S3
produces more but delivers approximately the same. Because Track A does
not expose downstream dispatch control to the agent, the active
constraint lies outside the controllable interface.

\hypertarget{43-track-b-extended-action-space-positive-result}{%
\subsection{4.3 Track B: extended action space (positive
result)}\label{43-track-b-extended-action-space-positive-result}}

Track B extends the action space from 5 to 7 dimensions by adding
downstream dispatch multipliers at Op10 and Op12, while preserving the
DES backbone and reward family. Table 9 presents the Track B results
from the production benchmark (500,000 timesteps, 5 seeds
\$\textbackslash times\$ 20 evaluation episodes).

PPO achieves fill rate 1.000 (zero backorders), cumulative reward
254.21, and order-level resilience (ReT mean) 0.950. The S2 baseline
with neutral dispatch (s2\_d1.00) reaches fill rate 0.966 and reward
178.24; the best static policy (S3 with double dispatch, s3\_d2.00)
reaches fill rate 0.988 and reward 171.06. PPO therefore exceeds
s2\_d1.00 by +3.40 percentage points in fill rate and the best static
policy by +1.23 percentage points. The reward gap versus the best static
policy is +83.16, reflecting both service improvement and cost
efficiency.

The learned policy's shift utilization is 78\% S1 / 16\% S2 / 6\%
S3-\/-\/-a marked contrast to static S2 (100\% S2) and static S3 (100\%
S3). This indicates that PPO learned to operate at the lowest assembly
cost (S1) for most of the episode while activating higher shifts
selectively when downstream conditions require surge capacity. The
combination of full fill rate with predominantly S1 operation represents
a cost-efficient strategy that no static policy achieves: static
S3+d2.00 reaches near-perfect fill but at permanently elevated assembly
cost, while PPO matches or exceeds its fill rate at substantially lower
average shift utilization.

\hypertarget{44-matched-ablation-5d-versus-7d}{%
\subsection{4.4 Matched ablation: 5D versus
7D}\label{44-matched-ablation-5d-versus-7d}}

To isolate the effect of downstream control from other differences
between Track A and Track B (observation dimensionality, risk profile),
we conduct a matched ablation holding all factors constant except the
action contract. Both conditions use observation version v7 (46
dimensions), reward ReT\_seq\_v1 (\$\textbackslash kappa = 0.20\$), risk
level adaptive\_benchmark\_v2, and a training budget of 100,000
timesteps across 3 seeds. The only difference is the action space: 5D
(Track A actions) versus 7D (Track A actions + Op10/Op12 dispatch).

Table 10 reports the results. Under the 5D condition, PPO reaches fill
rate 0.607, losing to static S2 (fill rate 0.616) by 0.9 percentage
points. Under the 7D condition, PPO reaches fill rate 0.964, winning by
+34.8 percentage points over the same S2 baseline. The fill-rate
difference between conditions is +35.7 percentage points. The reward
difference is +73.44.

Because the observation space, reward function, risk profile, and
training budget are identical across conditions, this matched comparison
provides strong evidence that the downstream control
dimensions-\/-\/-not the enriched observation space or the adaptive risk
profile alone-\/-\/-are materially responsible for the observed Track B
gain. The manuscript adopts careful language: this ablation
\emph{indicates} and \emph{supports the interpretation} that
action-space alignment with the active bottleneck determines RL
effectiveness in this benchmark. We do not claim universal causal proof
outside the frozen benchmark family, and we note that the ablation uses
a shorter training horizon (100k versus 500k) and fewer seeds (3 versus
5) than the production Track B run.

\hypertarget{45-summary}{%
\subsection{4.5 Summary}\label{45-summary}}

Table 11 provides a unified comparison across all conditions. The
experimental evidence forms a coherent narrative: Track A demonstrates a
structural limitation, Track B demonstrates that a minimal action-space
repair overcomes it, and the matched ablation localizes the effect to
the downstream control dimensions. The contribution is not that RL is
universally effective for supply chain resilience, but that its
effectiveness depends critically on whether the controllable interface
reaches the binding operational constraint.
