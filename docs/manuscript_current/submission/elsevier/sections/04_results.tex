\section{Results}\label{4-results}}

\hypertarget{41-des-validation}{%
\subsection{DES validation}\label{41-des-validation}}

The reconstructed DES was first checked against the deterministic
baseline (Cf0, single shift S=1) reported in Garrido-Rios (2017, Table
6.10)~\cite{garrido2017}. Using the official thesis basis of 8,064 hours per year (48
operational weeks), the simulation delivers 733,621 rations per year on
average, compared with the thesis reference of 767,592 rations, a
relative gap of $-4.43\%$. For context, the thesis's own correlated
inspection compares eight historical annual delivery totals against
simulated output, with individual annual gaps ranging from $-21.6\%$ to
$+14.1\%$ (RMSE $\approx$ 88,000 rations); our reconstruction gap sits
well inside that historically observed dispersion. We characterize the
environment as a thesis-grounded reconstruction with forensic workbook
replay and throughput checks --- software verification, exact
workbook-formula replay, and historical calibration --- rather than an
externally validated model of the operating supply chain. A secondary
Gregorian-basis check (8,760 hours per year) produces 796,940 rations
per year ($+3.82\%$ relative gap), confirming that the discrepancy is
driven by year-basis conventions rather than model error.

Under deterministic conditions with risks disabled, the simulation
produces 734,458 rations per year and delivers 733,621, with a fill rate
of 99.3\% and only 41 backorders accumulated over the 20-year horizon.
When stochastic risks are enabled at the current level, average annual
delivery falls to 677,750 rations ($-7.6\%$ relative to the
deterministic baseline), the fill rate drops to 68.3\%, and total
backorders rise to 1,825. Under the increased risk configuration used
for the benchmark, average annual delivery decreases further to 549,250
rations, fill rate falls to 45.6\%, and total backorders increase to
3,132. The DES therefore shows a clear transition from a well-calibrated
nominal system to a fragile network once disruption intensity escalates.

\hypertarget{42-track-a}{%
\subsection{Track A: thesis-grounded benchmark (negative
result)}\label{42-track-a}}

Under the thesis-grounded Track A contract, we first ask whether a
regime-conditioned oracle over the static decision space (inventory
buffer fraction and shift level) exposes any exploitable structure. A
dense grid search over the Track A buffer/shift family --- five risk
families, five frequency multipliers,
and three impact multipliers --- finds a real but small oracle headroom: the
best regime-varying combination of static actions beats the single best
constant policy by +0.000176 to +0.000296 Excel ReT (95\% CI lower bound
positive in the full grid), with the optimal action changing across
regimes. This headroom is small but not zero, so the relevant question is
whether a learned policy can convert it.

No tested learner does. Continuous PPO, PPO warm-started via behavior
cloning on the oracle action table, and PPO trained under a family-conflict
action contract (\texttt{per\_op\_conflict}, reward
\texttt{ReT\_excel\_plus\_cvar}) all fail to close the gap under
paired common-random-number evaluation against the same dense static
grid. The closest approach --- a single behavior-cloning-warm-started PPO
run under the family-conflict contract (3 training seeds, all three
converging to the same held-out value) --- misses the best static
comparator by
$-6.99\times10^{-6}$ Excel ReT (0.155247 vs.\ 0.155254), a difference two
orders of magnitude smaller than the measured oracle headroom, and is
Pareto-dominated by 10 static configurations on the joint Excel
ReT--resource frontier. Table~\ref{tab:track_a_boundary} summarizes the
boundary structure: measurable oracle headroom exists, and no tested
learner converts it. We therefore report Track A as a boundary
characterization: under dense common-random-number static frontiers and
the tested reward/algorithm combinations, no statistically and
operationally material dynamic-control
signal survives, even though a small regime-conditioned oracle headroom
is measurable. This is not claimed as a proof of mathematical
impossibility, only as the outcome of the tested learners under fair,
paired evaluation.

Track A and Track B report ReT on different numeric scales
($\approx$0.155 vs.\ $\approx$0.0059) because the two lanes use different
experiment families, risk regimes, and evaluation horizons. All claims in
this paper are \emph{within-lane} paired comparisons of a dynamic policy
against that lane's own static frontier; absolute ReT levels are never
compared across lanes.

The structural explanation is the downstream distribution bottleneck:
Op10--Op12 transport throughput ($\approx$2,500 rations/day)
approximately equals demand, so extra upstream production capacity from
higher shift levels yields sharply diminishing returns once a modest
buffer is in place. Because Track A does not expose downstream dispatch
control to the agent, the active constraint lies outside the
controllable interface --- motivating the Track B extension in
Section~4.3.

\hypertarget{43-track-b}{%
\subsection{Track B: extended action space (positive
result)}\label{43-track-b}}

Track B extends the action contract (\texttt{track\_b\_v1}, 8 dimensions)
by adding downstream dispatch multipliers at Op10 and Op12 on top of the
Track A buffer/shift variables, while preserving the DES backbone and
reward family (\texttt{control\_v1}). Table~\ref{tab:track_b_main} presents
the confirmatory 10-seed result (10 seeds $\times$ 60{,}000 timesteps
$\times$ 12 evaluation episodes, \texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2} risk
level, $h{=}104$ weeks), comparing the learned policy against the best
dense downstream-dispatch static comparator (\texttt{S2}, Op10$\times$2.00,
Op12$\times$1.50), selected from the 147-cell grid used in the canonical
5-seed dense-frontier audit and re-evaluated under paired
common-random-number (CRN) keys for the seed expansion.

PPO improves Garrido/Excel ReT by 0.000438 over the best dense
downstream-dispatch static comparator; the order-level ReT gain is
0.000426. Because the 120 CRN pairs reuse a finite set of evaluation
scenarios across seeds --- the per-seed evaluation plans overlap, so the
120 rows contain only 21 unique evaluation tapes --- we treat the training
seed as the inferential
unit: all ten per-seed mean deltas are individually positive (range
$+0.000408$ to $+0.000495$), each seed has 12/12 positive episode pairs,
and the seed-clustered bootstrap CI for the Excel ReT delta excludes zero.
\footnote{The 10-seed evidence bundle and seed-expansion verdict document in the project repository (Section~3.4).}

To remove the residual tape-dependence concern entirely, we additionally
ran a fully crossed held-out evaluation: all ten frozen checkpoints and
the prespecified static comparator were evaluated on the \emph{same} 60
fresh evaluation tapes, none of which appears in any training or prior
evaluation run. On this crossed design the advantage is, if anything,
slightly larger: Excel ReT $+0.000486$ with a two-way
(checkpoint~$\times$~tape) cluster-bootstrap CI95 of
$[+0.000456, +0.000517]$, all ten checkpoint means positive (range
$+0.000463$ to $+0.000509$), the delta positive on all 60 of 60 tapes,
and leave-one-out means never below $+0.000479$ when dropping any single
checkpoint or tape. The tail effect is proportionally larger still
(Excel ReT CVaR05 $+0.001637$, CI95 $[+0.001530, +0.001743]$). No single
checkpoint or evaluation scenario drives the result.
To interpret the magnitude: in this stress regime the recovery branch of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:ret} is empirically capped at $0.5/72 \approx 0.006944$
per order (Section~3.3),
so the gain moves the system from 78.6\% to 84.9\% of the attainable
metric ceiling --- approximately 6.3 percentage points of the operative
recovery-branch range. Operationally, the same policy produces
larger-magnitude
improvements in flow fill rate, rolling fill, and
recovery-tail cycle time (CTj p99 falls from 8{,}396 to 1{,}202 hours).
In operational terms, the slowest 1\% of rations orders under the best
static comparator take approximately 350 days to complete cycle time
(8{,}396~h $/$ 24); under PPO the same tail falls to approximately 50
days (1{,}202~h $/$ 24). This is a p99 tail statistic, not a median
delivery time and not a worst-case guarantee --- it describes the
99th-percentile resupply delay in the evaluated episodes --- but a
nearly sevenfold reduction in
that tail is operationally material for a forward food-resupply chain,
where the extreme-delay case is precisely the scenario a sustainment
planner must provision against.
The policy is Pareto non-dominated on the ReT--cost--tail--flow frontier:
no static policy in the dense dispatch grid jointly matches or exceeds PPO on Excel
ReT, cost, CTj p99, and flow fill simultaneously. It is \emph{not},
however, a strict cost win on the shift dimension: the shift-utilization
cost index is nominally lower for PPO in the 10-seed pool, but this
difference is not statistically distinguishable from zero (CI95 crosses
zero). We therefore report the result as non-dominated, not
shift-cost-dominant.

The local upstream bound does not overturn this result. Holding the best
downstream cell fixed (S2, Op10$\times$2.00, Op12$\times$1.50) and
varying Op3 and Op9 quantity multipliers over a 3$\times$3 grid, the best
static bound policy is Op3$\times$1.00, Op9$\times$1.25 with order-level
ReT 0.005612 (5-seed canonical pool). PPO remains higher at 0.005666 in
the same pool, a seed-paired delta of
+0.000054 (CI95 [+0.000042, +0.000066]). This is a local bound rather
than an exhaustive 8D static frontier, but it shows that the nearest
upstream static adjustment does not explain away the PPO advantage.

Because Track B's central lever is downstream dispatch, a cost account
that prices only assembly shifts would leave the intervention itself
uncosted. We therefore compute a dispatch-inclusive cost sensitivity from
the 10-seed CRN-paired episode ledgers (120 pairs):
$C(\lambda_d) = C_{\mathrm{shift}} + \lambda_d\,[(m_{10}-1)_+ + (m_{12}-1)_+]$,
where $m_{10}, m_{12}$ are the per-step mean Op10/Op12 dispatch
multipliers, $C_{\mathrm{shift}} = (\bar{s}-1)/2$ is the shift-utilization
index, and $\lambda_d$ is the charge per unit of dispatch expediting.
Table~\ref{tab:dispatch_cost} reports the result. Because the learned
policy expedites selectively (mean multipliers $\approx$1.30/1.27) while
the best static comparator holds aggressive multipliers permanently
(2.00/1.50), pricing dispatch \emph{favors} PPO: the 10-seed pool is
nominally cheaper even at $\lambda_d=0$ but not significantly so, and becomes
significantly cheaper (CI95 excluding zero) from $\lambda_d = 0.025$
upward. The resilience gain is therefore not
purchased through unpriced transport capacity; from $\lambda_d=0.025$
upward in this sensitivity grid, PPO is simultaneously
resilience-dominant and cheaper in total.

A companion masking experiment addresses whether this advantage could
instead reflect the learned policy exploiting privileged simulator
information --- specifically, a one-hot encoding of the true disruption
regime and forecast features derived from the true regime-transition
matrix, both present in the observation vector but not available to any
static comparator. Retraining PPO with these seven fields masked to zero
(Section~\ref{45-privileged-obs}) retains approximately 95\% of the
canonical advantage (+0.000395 to +0.000403 across two independent
training runs on separate infrastructure), and a non-learning policy given
direct access to the true regime signal (Section~\ref{45-privileged-obs})
still falls far short of PPO. The advantage is not an artifact of
privileged observation access.

\hypertarget{44-causal-ablation}{%
\subsection{Action-space decomposition (mechanism ablation)}\label{44-causal-ablation}}

To probe whether the Track B advantage reflects access to the downstream
bottleneck specifically, or simply a larger action space, we run a
5-seed $\times$ 60{,}000-timestep ablation over the 8-dimensional
\texttt{track\_b\_v1} contract, holding reward (\texttt{control\_v1}),
observation version (v7), and risk level (\texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2})
fixed. Three configurations are compared: \emph{joint} (the full 8D
contract); \emph{downstream\_only}, in which the shift dimension is frozen
at S2 while the policy retains the upstream inventory dimensions and
Op10/Op12 dispatch; and \emph{shift\_only}, in which the two dispatch
dimensions are frozen at their neutral multiplier while the policy retains
the upstream inventory dimensions and shift selection. Neither restricted
arm isolates a single lever --- both keep the upstream inventory
dimensions controllable --- so this decomposition contrasts
\emph{dispatch access} against \emph{shift access} on top of a shared
upstream contract, and cannot by itself attribute the gain causally to
downstream dispatch.

The gain does not scale with the number of controllable dimensions: the
arm retaining dispatch (with shift frozen) captures a \emph{larger}
advantage over its own best
evaluated comparator (+0.000429) than the full joint 8-dimensional contract
(+0.000367), with the shift-retaining arm capturing a comparable advantage
(+0.000377) to the joint contract. This is inconsistent with the simple
confound that Track B wins only because it gives the learner more
actuators. Because both restricted arms keep the upstream inventory
dimensions and each is scored against a different in-arm comparator,
this decomposition is supportive pattern evidence, not an identified
causal contrast for downstream dispatch access; the identified test is
the common-comparator factorial that follows.

\paragraph{Identified factorial.} To identify the causal value of dispatch
access we ran a corrected factorial with true nested contracts, one common
comparator, the canonical learning rate ($3\times10^{-4}$), and one common
held-out tape battery (24 fresh evaluation tapes never used in any training
or prior evaluation; 5 seeds $\times$ 60{,}000 timesteps per arm):
\emph{joint} (full 8D); \emph{upstream\_shift} (dispatch dimensions frozen
at the neutral multiplier --- upstream inventory and shift authority only);
and \emph{dispatch\_only} (upstream and shift dimensions frozen at neutral/S2
--- dispatch authority only). The pre-registered primary contrast, the value
of \emph{adding} Op10/Op12 dispatch authority to an otherwise identical
contract, is positive and identified: joint $-$ upstream\_shift
$= +0.000092$ Excel ReT, two-way (seed~$\times$~tape) cluster-bootstrap CI95
$[+0.000077, +0.000108]$, all five seeds positive. The full decomposition,
however, re-proportions the mechanism: against the common static comparator,
upstream\_shift alone gains $+0.000425$ $[+0.000372, +0.000488]$ ---
roughly four fifths of the joint contract's $+0.000517$
$[+0.000461, +0.000583]$ --- while dispatch\_only gains nothing
($-0.000012$ $[-0.000028, +0.000004]$). The latter is a coherence check
rather than a paradox: the dense 147-cell frontier already optimizes
exactly the shift$\times$dispatch subspace, so a learner restricted to that
subspace cannot beat it. The identified reading is therefore twofold:
adaptive value concentrates in closed-loop control of the levers the static
family holds fixed, and extending the contract to the downstream dispatch
bottleneck adds a further, causally identified increment on top. (The joint
arm, trained fresh for this factorial, independently replicates the
canonical headline on fresh tapes.) We retain the
joint 8D contract as the paper's canonical result (Section~4.3, trained at
full 60k$\times$5-seed scale with matched hyperparameters to Track A) and
treat the ablation as mechanism evidence rather than causal proof or a
recommendation to
drop the shift dimensions.\footnote{Two disclosed caveats: (i) the
ablation runs used learning rate
$1\times10^{-4}$, differing from the canonical run's $3\times10^{-4}$;
absolute ReT values are therefore not directly comparable to
Table~\ref{tab:track_b_main}, but the within-ablation comparison across
arms (identical hyperparameters in all three) is valid. (ii) The
downstream-only arm's static comparators partially collapse because the
frozen shift removes their distinguishing dimension; the heuristic
comparators in that arm act dynamically and remain genuine baselines,
which is why the reported deltas use the best \emph{evaluated} comparator
including heuristics.}

\hypertarget{45-privileged-obs}{%
\subsection{Ruling out privileged observation access}\label{45-privileged-obs}}

Observation version v7 includes, alongside operationally observable state
(backlog, queue pressure, rolling fill history), a one-hot encoding of the
simulator's true disruption regime and two forecast features derived from
the true regime-transition matrix. Because static and heuristic
comparators cannot condition on these fields, a skeptical reading of
Section~4.3 is that PPO's advantage reflects exploiting privileged
simulator state rather than learning genuine closed-loop control. We test
this from both directions.

\emph{Does PPO need the privileged fields?} We retrain PPO with the
regime one-hot and forecast fields masked to zero, holding all other
hyperparameters at their canonical values, and reproduce the run
independently on two separate compute environments. The masked policy
retains the great majority of the canonical advantage. Against the static
comparator family, the masked policy's order-level ReT advantage is
$+0.000403$ on the OVH replication and approximately $+0.000395$ in the
Kaggle replication, versus $+0.000415$ for the canonical, unmasked policy.
Including the strongest heuristic comparator narrows but does not remove
the margin; the OVH masked PPO mean is 0.005613 versus 0.005245 for the
best heuristic.

\emph{Does a non-learning policy with the same privileged access match
PPO?} We construct a regime-conditioned static lookup table --- the best
constant dispatch policy per observed disruption regime, fit directly on
the true regime signal PPO could in principle exploit --- and evaluate it,
six reactive dispatch heuristics, and 75 constant-dispatch policies,
CRN-paired against the canonical frozen PPO checkpoint. The fitted regime
table improves on the single best static constant by only $+0.0000277$,
a fraction of PPO's $+0.000427$ advantage over the same comparator; PPO
beats all 75 static candidates, all six heuristics, and the fitted regime
table individually, with 95\% confidence intervals excluding zero in
every one of 60 comparisons.

\emph{Is the forecast signal doing any work at all?} A third, sharper
test drops the two true-transition-matrix forecast fields entirely ---
training and evaluating with them masked --- and compares against the
full-v7 policy on the same fifteen training seeds under fixed-RNG paired
evaluation. The per-seed paired delta (no-forecast minus full-v7) on
Excel ReT is $+0.21\times 10^{-5}$ on average, with a 95\% confidence
interval of $[-1.26, +1.68]\times 10^{-5}$ that comfortably includes
zero and 9 of 15 seeds nominally favoring the \emph{no-forecast} arm
(Figure~\ref{fig:no_forecast_defense}). For scale, the headline
PPO-versus-static effect is $+43.8\times 10^{-5}$ --- roughly two
hundred times the entire range of forecast-removal deltas. Removing the
privileged forecast produces no measurable change in resilience.

Together these results indicate the Track B advantage is not explained by
access to privileged simulator state: PPO does not require it to win, a
non-learning policy that has it still cannot approach PPO's performance,
and deleting the forecast signal outright changes nothing measurable.

\hypertarget{46-reward-sweep}{%
\subsection{Reward sensitivity under Track B}\label{46-reward-sweep}}

To assess whether the Track B advantage depends on the specific choice of
training reward, we screen 18 reward/observation combinations under the
current 8-dimensional action contract: reward modes \texttt{control\_v1},
\texttt{ReT\_excel\_plus\_cvar} (tail weight $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.1,
0.2\}$), \texttt{ReT\_tail\_v2}, and \texttt{ReT\_garrido2024\_train},
crossed with observation versions v7, v8, and v9, each trained for
2 seeds $\times$ 40{,}000 timesteps (a screening budget, smaller than the
10-seed headline and the 5-seed mechanism checks) and evaluated
CRN-paired against the best dense static comparator.

All 18 cells show a positive Excel ReT delta
(range $+0.000195$ to $+0.000452$), and every cell registers a directional
win on the primary metric. This exploratory screen provides evidence that
the sign of the Track B effect is not uniquely tied to one reward
specification, CVaR tail weight, or observation version;
confirmatory-scale reward-level equivalence is not claimed. Two
chronology points matter for interpretation: the canonical
\texttt{control\_v1} contract was fixed \emph{before} the final dense-CRN
confirmatory audit, and the screen was conducted afterward as a
robustness check, not as a selection step for the headline result.
Promotion decisions within the screen selected 12 of 18 cells for further
scaling using a pre-stated threshold on Excel ReT and CVaR05 improvement;
all 18 outcomes, including the 6 non-promoted cells, are reported in the
evidence bundle. The uniform sign across 18 independently-trained
configurations is evidence against the concern that the canonical result
depends on a fortuitously tuned reward.

\hypertarget{48-prevention-boundary}{%
\subsection{Prevention boundary: the gain is adaptive recovery, not
anticipation}\label{48-prevention-boundary}}

The results so far establish \emph{that} downstream dispatch authority
converts into resilience; this section establishes \emph{when} the policy
earns it. A natural stronger claim would be anticipation: that the
learned policy prepares before disruption events rather than reacting to
them. We tested this claim adversarially and report the boundary
explicitly.

An initial event-aligned counterfactual --- replacing the policy's
actions in the four weeks before each disruption event with its own
``calm'' action and measuring the resilience drop --- appeared to show a
strong preventive signal (roughly three quarters of pre-event windows
carrying positive value, stable across seeds). Two negative controls
falsified the gate itself: a purely reactive baseline with no
anticipatory machinery of any kind passed it at an even higher rate, and
so did a variant trained on a temporally shuffled (content-free) risk
label. The artifact is instructive: under frequent overlapping
disruptions, the episode-level ``calm action'' statistic is contaminated
by legitimate reactive responses to \emph{other, nearby} events, so
substituting it into any pre-event window deletes real reactive value
and manufactures a positive delta for any sufficiently reactive policy.
A validated counterfactual with an event-exclusion halo around the calm
estimate returns both policies to the same null band (3--19\%
positive-pair rates, mean deltas one to two orders of magnitude smaller).

With the gate question settled, we measured the ceiling directly
(Figure~\ref{fig:prevention_ceiling}). First, a forced-preparation
response surface: for isolated real disruption events (and matched
event-free placebo anchors), we force the entire action vector to a
fixed posture --- calm, medium, or maximum preparation --- for the four
weeks before the anchor, then resume the policy, and score only the
orders causally exposed to that event. Across eight environment tiers
covering every risk family with a mechanically plausible preparation
channel (R22/R23 line-of-communication and forward-unit destruction,
R12/R13 upstream contract and supplier stalls, R21 natural disasters,
and a surge-inertia variant in which capacity changes take effect with a
lag --- precisely the physics that should reward preparation), the share
of real anchors where maximum preparation beats calm is 0--7\%,
indistinguishable from the placebo rate and far below any promotion
threshold; six of the eight tiers are \emph{exact} nulls, with
bit-identical exposed-order outcomes across all three postures while
resource cost varies. Second, a clairvoyant ceiling: a PPO trained
\emph{and} evaluated with the true future risk calendar visible in its
observation gains no resilience over the reactive baseline ($+9.83\%$
versus $+10.65\%$ over the best static under downstream stress) while
spending 19\% more resources. If perfect foreknowledge buys nothing, no
learned predictor can.

The mechanistic reading is consistent with the DES physics: the
disruption processes have exogenous, action-independent recovery
durations and knock out operations directly, so the dispatch and shift
levers cannot shorten an outage --- they can only move flow faster while
capacity exists. The Track B gain is therefore adaptive recovery and
exposure reduction, not anticipation, and we bound the claim
accordingly. Beyond the boundary itself, the episode is a methodological
caution we believe generalizes: event-aligned action-splice
counterfactuals are structurally fragile under frequent overlapping
disruptions, and a positive preventive signal from such a gate should
not be trusted until a reactive-only baseline, a content-free-label
control, and placebo anchors have all failed to reproduce it.

\hypertarget{49-summary}{%
\subsection{Summary}\label{49-summary}}

The experimental evidence forms a coherent narrative across seven result
dimensions. Track A demonstrates a structural limitation under
dense-frontier evaluation (Section~4.2). Track B demonstrates that an
operational action-space extension overcomes it under paired
common-random-number evaluation against a 147-cell dense
downstream-dispatch static grid across 10 training seeds, and remains
cheaper in total once dispatch expediting is priced at positive charges
(Section~4.3). A 5-seed identified factorial decomposes the mechanism:
most of the advantage over the dense static frontier comes from
closed-loop control of the upstream and shift levers the static family
holds fixed, adding downstream dispatch authority contributes a further
causally identified increment, and dispatch-only control does not beat
the frontier that already optimizes that subspace --- ruling out ``more
actuators'' as a sufficient explanation
(Section~4.4). Three independent tests --- an
obs-masked retrain, a privileged-access static comparator, and a 15-seed
paired forecast-removal comparison --- rule out
that the effect depends on the learned policy exploiting simulator-only
regime or forecast information (Section~4.5). A cross-regime stress
evaluation shows
the frozen policy transfers with fully positive seed-clustered CIs to
current and increased risk at both horizons, with the severe
service-floor regime disclosed as the boundary where PPO loses narrowly,
and a companion experiment
shows the disruption regime alone is not sufficient for a win: the
thesis-grounded Track A contract retrained on the same regime still loses
(Section~4.7). Finally, an adversarially
validated prevention audit bounds the mechanism in time: forced pre-event
preparation has no causal effect on event-exposed outcomes in any risk
family, and perfect foreknowledge buys no resilience --- the gain is
adaptive recovery, not anticipation
(Section~4.8). The central finding is not
that RL is universally effective, but that RL value is observed in this
benchmark when the
controllable interface reaches the operational bottleneck and the policy
can convert that authority into recovery performance.
