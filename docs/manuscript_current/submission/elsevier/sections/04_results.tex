\hypertarget{4-results}{%
\section{Results}\label{4-results}}

\hypertarget{41-des-validation}{%
\subsection{DES validation}\label{41-des-validation}}

The reconstructed DES was first validated against the deterministic
baseline (Cf0, single shift S=1) reported in Garrido-Rios (2017, Table
6.10)~\cite{garrido2017}. Using the official thesis basis of 8,064 hours per year (48
operational weeks), the simulation delivers 733,621 rations per year on
average, compared with the thesis reference of 767,592 rations, yielding
a relative gap of $-4.43\%$. This is comfortably within the accepted
$\pm 15\%$ validation threshold and supports the structural fidelity of
the rebuilt DES. A secondary Gregorian-basis check (8,760 hours per
year) produces 796,940 rations per year ($+3.82\%$ relative gap),
confirming that the discrepancy is driven by year-basis conventions
rather than model error.

Under deterministic conditions with risks disabled, the simulation
produces 734,458 rations per year and delivers 733,621, with a fill rate
of 99.3\% and only 41 backorders accumulated over the 20-year horizon.
When stochastic risks are enabled at the current level, average annual
delivery falls to 677,750 rations ($-7.6\%$ relative to the
deterministic baseline), the fill rate drops to 68.3\%, and total
backorders rise to 1,825. Under the increased risk configuration used
for the benchmark, average annual delivery decreases further to 549,250
rations, fill rate falls to 45.6\%, and total backorders increase to
3,132. The DES therefore shows a clear transition from a well-calibrated
nominal system to a fragile network once disruption intensity escalates.

\hypertarget{42-track-a}{%
\subsection{Track A: thesis-grounded benchmark (negative
result)}\label{42-track-a}}

Under the thesis-grounded Track A contract, we first ask whether a
regime-conditioned oracle over the static decision space (inventory
buffer fraction and shift level) exposes any exploitable structure. A
dense grid search over the Track A buffer/shift family --- five risk
families, five frequency multipliers,
and three impact multipliers --- finds a real but small oracle headroom: the
best regime-varying combination of static actions beats the single best
constant policy by +0.000176 to +0.000296 Excel ReT (95\% CI lower bound
positive in the full grid), with the optimal action changing across
regimes. This headroom is small but not zero, so the relevant question is
whether a learned policy can convert it.

No tested learner does. Continuous PPO, PPO warm-started via behavior
cloning on the oracle action table, and PPO trained under a family-conflict
action contract (\texttt{per\_op\_conflict}, reward
\texttt{ReT\_excel\_plus\_cvar}, 5 seeds) all fail to close the gap under
paired common-random-number evaluation against the same dense static
grid. The closest approach --- behavior-cloning-warm-started PPO under
the family-conflict contract --- misses the best static comparator by
$-6.99\times10^{-6}$ Excel ReT (0.155247 vs.\ 0.155254), a difference two
orders of magnitude smaller than the measured oracle headroom, and is
Pareto-dominated by 10 static configurations on the joint Excel
ReT--resource frontier. Table~\ref{tab:track_a_boundary} summarizes the
boundary structure: measurable oracle headroom exists, and no tested
learner converts it. We therefore report Track A as a boundary
characterization: under dense common-random-number static frontiers and
the tested reward/algorithm combinations, no statistically and
operationally material dynamic-control
signal survives, even though a small regime-conditioned oracle headroom
is measurable. This is not claimed as a proof of mathematical
impossibility, only as the outcome of the tested learners under fair,
paired evaluation.

Track A and Track B report ReT on different numeric scales
($\approx$0.155 vs.\ $\approx$0.0059) because the two lanes use different
experiment families, risk regimes, and evaluation horizons. All claims in
this paper are \emph{within-lane} paired comparisons of a dynamic policy
against that lane's own static frontier; absolute ReT levels are never
compared across lanes.

The structural explanation is the downstream distribution bottleneck:
Op10--Op12 transport throughput ($\approx$2,500 rations/day)
approximately equals demand, so extra upstream production capacity from
higher shift levels yields sharply diminishing returns once a modest
buffer is in place. Because Track A does not expose downstream dispatch
control to the agent, the active constraint lies outside the
controllable interface --- motivating the Track B extension in
Section~4.3.

dense downstream-dispatch static comparator (\texttt{S2}, Op10$\times$2.00,
Op12$\times$1.50), selected from the 147-cell grid used in the canonical
5-seed dense-frontier audit and re-evaluated under paired
common-random-number (CRN) keys for the seed expansion.

and \texttt{docs/TRACK\_B\_SEED\_EXPANSION\_6\_10\_VERDICT\_2026-07-02.md}.}
To interpret the magnitude: in this stress regime the
Excel ReT recovery branch is bounded above by $0.5/72 \approx 0.006944$,
so the gain moves the system from 78.6\% to 84.9\% of the attainable
metric ceiling --- approximately 6.3 percentage points of the operative
recovery-branch range. Operationally, the same policy produces
larger-magnitude
improvements in flow fill rate, rolling fill, and
recovery-tail cycle time (CTj p99 falls from 8{,}396 to 1{,}202 hours).
The policy is Pareto non-dominated on the ReT--cost--tail--flow frontier:
no static policy in the dense dispatch grid jointly matches or exceeds PPO on Excel
ReT, cost, CTj p99, and flow fill simultaneously. It is \emph{not},
however, a strict cost win on the shift dimension: the shift-utilization
cost index is nominally lower for PPO in the 10-seed pool, but this
difference is not statistically distinguishable from zero (CI95 crosses
zero). We therefore report the result as non-dominated, not
shift-cost-dominant.

The local upstream bound does not overturn this result. Holding the best
downstream cell fixed (S2, Op10$\times$2.00, Op12$\times$1.50) and
varying Op3 and Op9 quantity multipliers over a 3$\times$3 grid, the best
static bound policy is Op3$\times$1.00, Op9$\times$1.25 with order-level
ReT 0.005612 (5-seed canonical pool). PPO remains higher at 0.005666 in
the same pool, a seed-paired delta of
+0.000054 (CI95 [+0.000042, +0.000066]). This is a local bound rather
than an exhaustive 8D static frontier, but it shows that the nearest
upstream static adjustment does not explain away the PPO advantage.

Because Track B's central lever is downstream dispatch, a cost account
that prices only assembly shifts would leave the intervention itself
uncosted. We therefore compute a dispatch-inclusive cost sensitivity from
the canonical 5-seed CRN-paired episode ledgers (60 pairs):
$C(\lambda) = C_{\mathrm{shift}} + \lambda\,[(m_{10}-1)_+ + (m_{12}-1)_+]$,
where $m_{10}, m_{12}$ are the per-step mean Op10/Op12 dispatch
multipliers and $\lambda$ is the charge per unit of dispatch expediting.
Table~\ref{tab:dispatch_cost} reports the result. Because the learned
policy expedites selectively (mean multipliers $\approx$1.30/1.27) while
the best static comparator holds aggressive multipliers permanently
(2.00/1.50), pricing dispatch \emph{favors} PPO: the 10-seed pool is
nominally cheaper even at $\lambda=0$ but not significantly so, and becomes
significantly cheaper (CI95 excluding zero) from $\lambda = 0.025$
upward. The resilience gain is therefore not
purchased through unpriced transport capacity; from $\lambda=0.025$
upward in this sensitivity grid, PPO is simultaneously
resilience-dominant and cheaper in total.

A companion masking experiment addresses whether this advantage could
instead reflect the learned policy exploiting privileged simulator
information --- specifically, a one-hot encoding of the true disruption
regime and forecast features derived from the true regime-transition
matrix, both present in the observation vector but not available to any
static comparator. Retraining PPO with these seven fields masked to zero
(Section~\ref{45-privileged-obs}) retains approximately 95\% of the
canonical advantage (+0.000395 to +0.000403 across two independent
training runs on separate infrastructure), and a non-learning policy given
direct access to the true regime signal (Section~\ref{45-privileged-obs})
still falls far short of PPO. The advantage is not an artifact of
privileged observation access.

\hypertarget{44-causal-ablation}{%
\subsection{Action-space decomposition (mechanism ablation)}\label{44-causal-ablation}}

To test whether the Track B advantage reflects access to the downstream
bottleneck specifically, or simply a larger action space, we run a
5-seed $\times$ 60{,}000-timestep ablation over the 8-dimensional
\texttt{track\_b\_v1} contract, holding reward (\texttt{control\_v1}),
observation version (v7), and risk level (\texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2})
fixed. Three configurations are compared: \emph{joint} (the full 8D
contract), \emph{downstream\_only} (shift frozen at S2, only Op10/Op12
dispatch controllable), and \emph{shift\_only} (dispatch frozen at neutral,
only shift controllable).

The gain does not scale with the number of controllable dimensions: a
downstream-only policy captures a \emph{larger} advantage over its own best
evaluated comparator (+0.000429) than the full joint 8-dimensional contract
(+0.000367), with shift-only capturing a comparable advantage
(+0.000377) to the joint contract. This rules out the confound that Track
B wins simply because it gives the learner more actuators. Downstream
dispatch access is \emph{sufficient} to recover the largest observed
in-arm advantage and is the strongest single lever in this decomposition;
shift-only control also captures part of the dynamic-control effect, so
we do not claim downstream dispatch is the only useful control surface ---
both dimensions provide feedback levers, with downstream access strongest
in this design. We retain the
joint 8D contract as the paper's canonical result (Section~4.3, trained at
full 60k$\times$5-seed scale with matched hyperparameters to Track A) and
treat the ablation as mechanism evidence rather than causal proof or a
recommendation to
drop the shift dimensions.\footnote{Two disclosed caveats: (i) the
ablation runs used learning rate

absolute ReT values are therefore not directly comparable to
Table~\ref{tab:track_b_main}, but the within-ablation comparison across
arms (identical hyperparameters in all three) is valid. (ii) The
downstream-only arm's static comparators partially collapse because the
frozen shift removes their distinguishing dimension; the heuristic
comparators in that arm act dynamically and remain genuine baselines,
which is why the reported deltas use the best \emph{evaluated} comparator
including heuristics.}

\hypertarget{45-privileged-obs}{%
\subsection{Ruling out privileged observation access}\label{45-privileged-obs}}

Observation version v7 includes, alongside operationally observable state
(backlog, queue pressure, rolling fill history), a one-hot encoding of the
simulator's true disruption regime and two forecast features derived from
the true regime-transition matrix. Because static and heuristic
comparators cannot condition on these fields, a skeptical reading of
Section~4.3 is that PPO's advantage reflects exploiting privileged
simulator state rather than learning genuine closed-loop control. We test
this from both directions.

\emph{Does PPO need the privileged fields?} We retrain PPO with the
regime one-hot and forecast fields masked to zero, holding all other
hyperparameters at their canonical values, and reproduce the run
independently on two separate compute environments. The masked policy
retains the great majority of the canonical advantage. Against the static
comparator family, the masked policy's order-level ReT advantage is
$+0.000403$ on the OVH replication and approximately $+0.000395$ in the
Kaggle replication, versus $+0.000415$ for the canonical, unmasked policy.
Including the strongest heuristic comparator narrows but does not remove
the margin; the OVH masked PPO mean is 0.005613 versus 0.005245 for the
best heuristic.

\emph{Does a non-learning policy with the same privileged access match
PPO?} We construct a regime-conditioned static lookup table --- the best
constant dispatch policy per observed disruption regime, fit directly on
the true regime signal PPO could in principle exploit --- and evaluate it,
six reactive dispatch heuristics, and 75 constant-dispatch policies,
CRN-paired against the canonical frozen PPO checkpoint. The fitted regime
table improves on the single best static constant by only $+0.0000277$,
a fraction of PPO's $+0.000427$ advantage over the same comparator; PPO
beats all 75 static candidates, all six heuristics, and the fitted regime
table individually, with 95\% confidence intervals excluding zero in
every one of 60 comparisons.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fig3_gap_decomposition}

Together these results indicate the Track B advantage is not explained by
access to privileged simulator state: PPO does not require it to win, and
a non-learning policy that has it still cannot approach PPO's
performance.

\hypertarget{46-reward-sweep}{%
\subsection{Reward sensitivity under Track B}\label{46-reward-sweep}}

To assess whether the Track B advantage depends on the specific choice of
training reward, we screen 18 reward/observation combinations under the
current 8-dimensional action contract: reward modes \texttt{control\_v1},
\texttt{ReT\_excel\_plus\_cvar} (tail weight $\alpha \in \{0.05, 0.1,
0.2\}$), \texttt{ReT\_tail\_v2}, and \texttt{ReT\_garrido2024\_train},
crossed with observation versions v7, v8, and v9, each trained for
2 seeds $\times$ 40{,}000 timesteps (a screening budget, smaller than the
10-seed headline and the 5-seed mechanism checks) and evaluated
CRN-paired against the best dense static comparator.

All 18 cells show a positive Excel ReT delta
(range $+0.000195$ to $+0.000452$), and every cell registers a directional
win on the primary metric. This exploratory screen provides evidence that
the sign of the Track B effect is not uniquely tied to one reward
specification, CVaR tail weight, or observation version;
confirmatory-scale reward-level equivalence is not claimed. Two
chronology points matter for interpretation: the canonical
\texttt{control\_v1} contract was fixed \emph{before} the final dense-CRN
confirmatory audit, and the screen was conducted afterward as a
robustness check, not as a selection step for the headline result.
Promotion decisions within the screen selected 12 of 18 cells for further
scaling using a pre-stated threshold on Excel ReT and CVaR05 improvement;
all 18 outcomes, including the 6 non-promoted cells, are reported in the
evidence bundle. The uniform sign across 18 independently-trained
configurations is evidence against the concern that the canonical result
depends on a fortuitously tuned reward.

As a further check on whether the disruption regime alone, independent of
the action space, explains the Track B result, we retrain the original
Track A action family (buffer/shift only) directly on
\texttt{adaptive\_benchmark\_v2} --- the same stress regime used for the
Track B canonical result --- at matched scale (5 seeds $\times$ 60{,}000
timesteps). The retrained Track A policy does not outperform the best
constant-fraction static comparator on this regime (Excel ReT 0.00530 vs.\
0.00539; CVaR also favors the static). Access to the stress regime is
therefore not, by itself, sufficient for a learned policy to win; the
result additionally requires action-space coverage of the downstream
bottleneck.

\hypertarget{48-summary}{%
\subsection{Summary}\label{48-summary}}

The experimental evidence forms a coherent narrative across six result
dimensions. Track A demonstrates a structural limitation under
dense-frontier evaluation (Section~4.2). Track B demonstrates that an
operational action-space extension overcomes it under paired
common-random-number evaluation against a 147-cell dense
downstream-dispatch static grid across 10 training seeds, and remains
cheaper in total once dispatch expediting is priced at positive charges
(Section~4.3). A 5-seed action-space decomposition shows downstream
dispatch access is sufficient to recover the largest in-arm advantage,
ruling out ``more actuators''
as the explanation (Section~4.4). Two independent tests --- an
obs-masked retrain and a privileged-access static comparator --- rule out
that the effect depends on the learned policy exploiting simulator-only
regime information (Section~4.5). A cross-regime stress evaluation shows
the frozen policy transfers with fully positive seed-clustered CIs to
current and increased risk at both horizons, with the severe
service-floor regime disclosed as the boundary where PPO loses narrowly,
