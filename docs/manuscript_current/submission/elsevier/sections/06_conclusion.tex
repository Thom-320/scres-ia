\section{6. Conclusion}\label{6-conclusion}}

This paper presented a reproducible DES+RL benchmark for operational
resilience control in a military food supply chain, grounded in
Garrido-Rios' validated 13-operation simulation model~\cite{garrido2017}. The
experimental design uses two matched benchmark families to answer a
structural question: when does reinforcement learning outperform strong
static baselines in resilience-oriented operational control?

Under the thesis-faithful Track A contract, where the agent controls
upstream inventory and assembly shifts, dense common-random-number
static frontiers and multiple RL configurations across reward modes,
observation versions, and algorithms leave no publishable dynamic-control
advantage. The root cause is structural: the active downstream
distribution bottleneck lies outside the agent's controllable interface,
limiting the marginal value of upstream optimization regardless of
algorithmic sophistication.

Under the extended Track B contract, which adds downstream transport
dispatch at Op10 and Op12 (an 8-dimensional action contract) while
preserving the DES backbone, PPO improves Garrido/Excel resilience over
the best of a dense grid of static comparators under paired
common-random-number evaluation (+0.000426, 95\% CI entirely positive),
with matching gains in service continuity and recovery-tail metrics. A
5-seed action-space ablation shows the gain concentrates in
downstream-dispatch access rather than in action-space size; the
advantage holds for current and increased Garrido-native risk levels at
both evaluated horizons, while severe risk remains mixed; and two
independent tests --- an observation-masked retrain and a
privileged-access static comparator --- rule out that the effect depends
on the learned policy exploiting simulator-only regime information
unavailable to any static or heuristic baseline.

The central lesson is that RL effectiveness in this benchmark depends
not on algorithmic novelty or reward engineering but on whether the
controllable interface reaches the binding operational constraint, and
that this dependence survives adversarial checks for the two most common
alternative explanations --- larger action spaces and privileged
observation access. This finding connects bottleneck reasoning from the
Theory of Constraints with RL action-space design---a connection that, to
our knowledge, has not been demonstrated empirically in the supply chain
literature. For practitioners and researchers designing RL-based supply
chain control systems, the implication is direct: before investing in
architectural complexity, verify that the agent can act on the
constraint that actually limits system performance.
