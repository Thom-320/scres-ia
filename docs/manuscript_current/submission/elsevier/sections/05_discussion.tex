\section{5. Discussion}\label{5-discussion}}

\hypertarget{51-interpreting-track-a}{%
\subsection{5.1 Interpreting the Track A negative result}\label{51-interpreting-track-a}}

The Track A result should be read as evidence of a structural limitation
rather than evidence that RL is ineffective for supply chains in general.
Under the thesis-faithful contract, the static S2 policy already operates
near the practical ceiling imposed by downstream distribution capacity:
Op10--Op12 transport throughput of approximately 2,500 rations per day
roughly matches demand, so additional upstream production capacity from S3
generates excess inventory in intermediate buffers without proportionally
increasing deliveries. A design-of-experiments analysis confirms an
asymmetric action landscape: destructive inventory settings reduce reward by
44--65\%, while positive headroom above S2-neutral is limited to
approximately 1\%. PPO's convergence to near-neutral inventory actions with
S2-region shift selection is therefore rational risk management rather than
learning failure. Reward redesign (14 modes tested), memory (RecurrentPPO),
and richer observations (v2--v6) do not overturn this structural limitation
because the binding constraint lies outside the controllable interface.

\hypertarget{52-what-track-b-changes}{%
\subsection{5.2 What Track B changes}\label{52-what-track-b-changes}}

Track B changes the controllable interface, not the underlying DES physics.
By adding Op10 and Op12 dispatch multipliers to the action space, the agent
gains leverage over the downstream bottleneck that constrains Track A. The
learned policy exploits this by running predominantly at S1 (77\% of
steps)---the lowest-cost assembly configuration---while modulating downstream
dispatch to maintain perfect fill. This shift mix is a strategy that no
static policy can replicate: static S3($d{=}2.0$) achieves near-perfect fill
but at permanently elevated assembly cost, while PPO matches or exceeds it
with 57\% fewer assembly hours.

The causal ablation (Section 4.4) strengthens this interpretation at
production scale. With 500,000 timesteps and 5 seeds, the
\emph{shift\_only} condition reproduces Track A's failure (fill = 0.953,
below the best static), while \emph{downstream\_only} reproduces Track B's
success (fill = 1.000). The \emph{joint} configuration adds cost efficiency
but not fill improvement. This three-way decomposition provides causal
evidence that downstream dispatch---not shifts, not observation enrichment,
not risk profile changes---is the materially responsible dimension.

\hypertarget{53-action-space-alignment}{%
\subsection{5.3 Action-space alignment as a design principle}\label{53-action-space-alignment}}

The dual-track result connects three literatures that have not previously
been linked empirically: the Theory of Constraints \cite{goldratt1984goal},
which identifies the binding constraint as the lever that determines system
throughput; RL agent design, which determines what the agent can control; and
action-space architecture, which determines how decisions are represented.
Our benchmark demonstrates a concrete instance of this connection: when the
action space does not cover the active constraint (Track A), better
algorithms and rewards cannot compensate; when it does (Track B), a standard
PPO+MLP agent produces decisive advantage. The reward sweep (Section 4.5)
reinforces this: 5 of 7 reward formulations converge to the same strong
policy, whereas in Track A no reward formulation succeeds. This suggests a
practical design principle: \emph{before investing in architectural novelty
or reward engineering, verify that the agent's controllable interface reaches
the binding operational bottleneck.}

\hypertarget{54-positioning}{%
\subsection{5.4 Positioning relative to the AI--SCRES literature}\label{54-positioning}}

Recent work at the intersection of AI and supply chain resilience spans
several paradigms. Rajagopal and Sivasakthivel (2024) use weighted
feed-forward neural networks for resilience \emph{strategy selection}---a
classification task fundamentally different from our online adaptive control.
Rezki and Mansouri (2024) apply neural networks to \emph{predict} supplier
risk, while Ordibazar et al.\ (2022) use counterfactual explanations for
risk \emph{recommendation}. These contributions address complementary
problems (prediction, classification, explanation) rather than the
prescriptive operational control explored here. Kogler and Maxera (2026)
review 72 papers combining DES with ML for supply chain analysis,
confirming that DES+RL is the dominant hybrid paradigm; our work contributes
a specific instance with validated thesis-grounded data, honest negative
results, and causal ablation evidence that is rare in this literature.

Among RL-specific competitors, Ding et al.\ (2026) apply MAPPO for dynamic
reconfiguration of supply chain topology under disruption, operating at the
strategic level with abstract network actions. Our single-agent approach
asks a complementary question at the operational level: given a fixed
topology, does expanding operational control scope change the outcome? The
Track B result suggests that when the bottleneck can be identified and
controlled by a single agent, centralized control with appropriate action
scope suffices.

\hypertarget{55-limitations}{%
\subsection{5.5 Limitations}\label{55-limitations}}

Several limitations bound the generalizability of these findings. First, the
benchmark is one validated military supply chain; generalization to other
topologies, demand patterns, or organizational structures requires further
investigation. Second, the observation ablation was conducted at 50,000
timesteps (3 seeds), a reduced budget compared to the main experiments;
while preliminary results suggest observation design matters less than
action-space alignment, stronger replication would increase confidence.
Third, the problem is better described as partially observed control than a
fully identified MDP: the agent observes a projection of the simulation
state, not the full internal state. Fourth, the paper uses PPO exclusively
(with RecurrentPPO as a comparator); other algorithms (SAC, TD3) were not
tested, though the reward-insensitivity finding suggests algorithmic choice
is secondary to action-space alignment in this regime. Fifth,
ReT\_seq\_v1 is a training reward designed for gradient quality, not a
theoretically complete resilience index; the paper-facing reporting
therefore uses multiple complementary metrics. Finally, Track B's
adaptive\_benchmark\_v2 risk profile differs from Track A's increased
profile; the matched ablation controls for this, but the production
comparison across tracks conflates action-contract and risk-profile changes.

\hypertarget{56-future-work}{%
\subsection{5.6 Future work}\label{56-future-work}}

Three directions emerge. First, the action-space alignment principle should
be tested on other validated supply chain DES models to assess
generalizability beyond the MFSC topology. Second, more expressive policy
architectures---including attention-based networks, graph neural networks,
and emerging KAN-based designs as proposed by Garrido et al.\
(2024b)---remain relevant for supply chains with variable topology or
multi-agent coordination, but were excluded from this paper because the
standard PPO+MLP architecture already achieves fill rate 1.000 under Track
B. Third, training specialized policies for extreme scenarios
(severe, severe\_extended) rather than relying on the increased-risk-trained
policy could establish tighter upper bounds on RL effectiveness under
catastrophic disruptions. The observation ablation at 50k suggests that
observation design has a secondary effect; scaling this to 500k would
strengthen the conclusion.
