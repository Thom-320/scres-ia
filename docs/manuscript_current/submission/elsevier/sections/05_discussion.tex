\hypertarget{5-discussion}{%
\section{5. Discussion}\label{5-discussion}}

\hypertarget{51-interpreting-the-track-a-negative-result}{%
\subsection{5.1 Interpreting the Track A negative
result}\label{51-interpreting-the-track-a-negative-result}}

The Track A result should be read as evidence of a structural limitation
rather than as evidence that RL is ineffective for supply chains in
general. Under the thesis-faithful contract, the static S2 policy
already operates near the practical ceiling imposed by downstream
distribution capacity: Op10-\/-Op12 transport throughput of
approximately 2,500 rations per day roughly matches demand, so
additional upstream production capacity from S3 generates excess
inventory in intermediate buffers without proportionally increasing
deliveries. The design-of-experiments analysis confirms an asymmetric
action landscape in which destructive inventory settings reduce reward
by 44-\/-65\% while positive headroom above S2-neutral is limited to
approximately 1\%. In this regime, PPO's convergence to near-neutral
inventory actions with S2-region shift selection is rational risk
management rather than learning failure. Reward redesign (14 modes
tested), memory (RecurrentPPO), and richer observations (v2-\/-v6) do
not overturn this structural limitation because the binding constraint
lies outside the controllable interface.

\hypertarget{52-what-track-b-changes}{%
\subsection{5.2 What Track B changes}\label{52-what-track-b-changes}}

Track B changes the controllable interface, not the underlying DES
physics. The positive result therefore supports a diagnosis about
control scope rather than a story about algorithmic sophistication. By
adding Op10 and Op12 dispatch multipliers to the action space, the agent
gains leverage over the downstream bottleneck that constrains Track A.
The learned policy exploits this by running predominantly at S1 (78\% of
steps)-\/-\/-the lowest-cost assembly configuration-\/-\/-while
modulating downstream dispatch to maintain perfect fill. This shift mix
is a strategy that no static policy can replicate: static S3+d2.00
achieves near-perfect fill but at permanently elevated assembly cost,
while PPO matches or exceeds it at substantially lower average capacity
utilization. The cost-efficiency implication is that the agent learned
not just \emph{what} to deliver but \emph{how cheaply} to deliver it
when downstream flows are controllable.

\hypertarget{53-action-space-alignment-as-a-design-principle}{%
\subsection{5.3 Action-space alignment as a design
principle}\label{53-action-space-alignment-as-a-design-principle}}

The dual-track result connects three literatures that have not
previously been linked empirically: the Theory of Constraints
(Goldratt), which identifies the binding constraint as the lever that
determines system throughput; RL agent design, which determines what the
agent can control; and action-space architecture, which determines how
decisions are represented. Our benchmark demonstrates a concrete
instance of this connection: when the action space does not cover the
active constraint (Track A), better algorithms and rewards cannot
compensate; when it does (Track B), a standard PPO+MLP agent produces
decisive advantage. This suggests a practical design principle for
RL-for-SCRES applications: before investing in architectural novelty or
reward engineering, verify that the agent's controllable interface
reaches the binding operational bottleneck.

\hypertarget{54-limitations}{%
\subsection{5.4 Limitations}\label{54-limitations}}

Several limitations bound the generalizability of these findings. First,
the matched ablation uses 100,000 training timesteps and 3 seeds, a
shorter budget than the production Track B benchmark (500,000 timesteps,
5 seeds). While the effect size is large (+35.7 percentage points in
fill rate), stronger replication would increase confidence. Second, the
Track B risk profile (adaptive\_benchmark\_v2) differs from Track A's
(increased), so the production comparison across tracks conflates
action-contract changes with risk-profile changes. The matched ablation
addresses this by holding the risk profile constant, but the 100k budget
remains a limitation. Third, the problem is better described as
partially observed control than a fully identified MDP: the agent
observes a projection of the simulation state, not the full internal
state. Fourth, the benchmark is one validated military supply chain, not
a universal SCRES environment; generalization to other topologies,
demand patterns, or organizational structures requires further
investigation. Finally, ReT\_seq\_v1 is a training reward designed for
gradient quality, not a theoretically complete resilience index; the
paper-facing reporting therefore uses multiple complementary metrics
rather than a single reward-based ranking.

\hypertarget{55-future-work}{%
\subsection{5.5 Future work}\label{55-future-work}}

Three directions emerge from this benchmark. First, the ablation should
be scaled to production-grade replication (500k timesteps, 10+ seeds)
with formal statistical testing to strengthen the causal interpretation.
Second, the action-space alignment principle should be tested on other
validated supply chain DES models to assess whether the finding
generalizes beyond the MFSC topology. Third, more expressive policy
architectures-\/-\/-including attention-based networks, graph neural
networks, and emerging KAN-based designs-\/-\/-remain relevant for
supply chains with variable topology or multi-agent coordination, but
they were deliberately excluded from this paper because the standard
PPO+MLP architecture already achieves fill rate 1.000 under Track B.
Architectural extension is future work, not a requirement of the present
benchmark.
