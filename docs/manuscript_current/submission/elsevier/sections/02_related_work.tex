\hypertarget{21-reinforcement-learning-in-logistics-and-supply-chain-management}{%
\subsection{2.1 Reinforcement learning in logistics and supply chain
management}\label{21-reinforcement-learning-in-logistics-and-supply-chain-management}}

Reinforcement learning has become an established paradigm for adaptive
decision-making in supply chain management. Yan et al. (2022) provide a
comprehensive review covering methodologies, state of the art, and
opportunities for RL in logistics and SCM, documenting applications
across inventory ordering, vehicle routing, and warehouse operations.
Rolf et al. (2023) offer a semi-systematic review classifying RL
algorithms and applications in SCM, noting that Q-learning variants have
dominated historically but that policy-gradient methods such as PPO and
A3C are gaining traction. Boute et al. (2022) establish a design roadmap
for deep RL in inventory control, systematizing key choices around state
representation, action formulation, and reward engineering. Powell
(2022) provides the theoretical backdrop, arguing that RL's
value-function approximation is only one of four policy classes and that
simpler policy representations often outperform neural approaches in
structured operations problems. These reviews confirm that RL in SCM is
a mature field; our paper does not claim novelty in the \emph{idea} of
applying RL to supply chains but rather contributes a benchmark-centered
diagnosis of when RL succeeds and when it does not.

\hypertarget{22-discrete-event-simulation-and-machine-learning-for-supply-chains}{%
\subsection{2.2 Discrete-event simulation and machine learning for
supply
chains}\label{22-discrete-event-simulation-and-machine-learning-for-supply-chains}}

The integration of discrete-event simulation with machine learning has
accelerated sharply in recent years. Rolf et al. (2025) review 72 papers
combining DES with ML for supply chain analysis, finding that 82\% were
published in the last five years and that RL is the most frequent ML
paradigm in these hybrid pipelines. Perez-Pena et al. (2025) develop a
classification framework applied to 99 simulation-ML papers, revealing
that optimization is rarely incorporated into hybrid workflows. Our work
sits squarely in this family: a custom SimPy DES wrapped with a
Gymnasium interface for RL training. What distinguishes this benchmark
from the typical DES+RL study is its grounding in a validated PhD thesis
model (Garrido-Rios, 2017) rather than a synthetic or stylized
environment, and its explicit treatment of control-contract design as
the central experimental variable.

\hypertarget{23-rl-for-operational-and-inventory-control}{%
\subsection{2.3 RL for operational and inventory
control}\label{23-rl-for-operational-and-inventory-control}}

The dominant application of RL in supply chains remains inventory and
operational cost control. Gijsbrechts et al. (2022) apply A3C to
lost-sales, dual-sourcing, and multi-echelon problems, achieving
3-\/-6\% optimality gaps but failing to consistently outperform capped
base-stock policies. De Moor et al. (2022) demonstrate that
potential-based reward shaping can stabilize DRL training for perishable
inventory but does not always help the agent surpass teacher heuristics.
Dehaybe et al. (2024) train PPO on non-stationary demand using rolling
forecast horizons and achieve near-optimal lot-sizing policies. Geevers
et al. (2024) apply PPO with continuous actions via Stable-Baselines3 to
multi-echelon networks, reporting 6.6-\/-16.4\% cost reductions over
classical benchmarks. Temizoz et al. (2025) propose Deep Controlled
Learning, achieving optimality gaps below 0.2\% and consistently
outperforming all DRL baselines and heuristics. Vanvuchelen et al.
(2025) address action-space scalability through continuous
representations, finding that discrete formulations fail to scale to
high-dimensional inventory problems.

These papers collectively show that the primary challenge for RL in
inventory control is not architectural novelty but careful formulation
of the MDP-\/-\/-state representation, reward design, and action-space
structure. Our work extends this insight in a different direction: we
show that even with a well-designed reward and a standard PPO algorithm,
RL fails when the action space does not cover the binding operational
constraint. The issue in Track A is not algorithmic weakness or reward
misspecification but structural bottleneck misalignment.

\hypertarget{24-supply-chain-resilience-reconfiguration-and-marl}{%
\subsection{2.4 Supply chain resilience, reconfiguration, and
MARL}\label{24-supply-chain-resilience-reconfiguration-and-marl}}

The closest competitor in the resilience literature is Ding et al.
(2026), who apply MAPPO to dynamic reconfiguration of a supply chain
system-of-systems under disruption risks, with macro-level actions
(filling, repairing, recruiting) operating on an abstract network
topology. Their contribution is strategic topology redesign; ours is
operational control within a fixed, detailed DES. The distinction is not
cosmetic: their state space represents network-level attributes (node
density, clustering coefficients), while ours represents operational
variables (inventory levels, disruption flags, shift configuration) at
hourly granularity. Rolf et al. (2024) study supply chain recovery under
disruptions using system dynamics coupled with RL, demonstrating that RL
can learn robust order policies without historical data. Kim (2024)
proposes cooperative MARL for inventory transshipment during
disruptions. Liu et al. (2025) apply heterogeneous-agent PPO (HAPPO) to
decentralized multi-echelon inventory, finding that hybrid rewards
outperform purely self-interested or cooperative objectives. Kotecha and
del Rio Chanona (2025) combine graph neural networks with MAPPO for
inventory control in supply chain networks.

These MARL and resilience papers address coordination across
organizational boundaries or topology reconfiguration. Our single-agent
approach asks a simpler but complementary question: given a fixed supply
chain topology, does expanding the agent's operational control scope to
include the active bottleneck change the benchmark outcome? The Track B
result suggests that when the bottleneck can be identified and
controlled by a single agent, centralized control with appropriate
action scope suffices.

\hypertarget{25-positioning-summary}{%
\subsection{2.5 Positioning summary}\label{25-positioning-summary}}

This paper does not claim to be the first RL-for-SCM or DES+RL study.
Its contribution is a thesis-grounded benchmark and structural diagnosis
showing that RL success in resilience control depends on action-space
alignment with the active operational constraint-\/-\/-a finding that
connects bottleneck reasoning from the Theory of Constraints (Goldratt)
with RL agent design in a way that, to our knowledge, has not been
demonstrated empirically in the published literature.
