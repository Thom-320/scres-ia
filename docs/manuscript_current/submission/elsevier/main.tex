\title{When Does Reinforcement Learning Work for Supply Chain Resilience? Action-Space Alignment in a Military DES Benchmark}
\begin{abstract}
Supply chain resilience research increasingly uses reinforcement learning and hybrid simulation workflows, but much of the literature still emphasizes inventory cost optimization, abstract network reconfiguration, or generic benchmark environments rather than operational control on validated, domain-specific models. We present a reproducible discrete-event simulation plus reinforcement learning benchmark for resilience control in a 13-operation military food supply chain rebuilt from Garrido-Rios' thesis and exposed through a Gymnasium-compatible interface. Under the thesis-faithful buffer/shift decision family (Track A), dense common-random-number static frontiers leave no publishable dynamic-control advantage: no tested learner converts the small measured headroom over the best static policy. We then introduce an operational extension (Track B) that adds downstream dispatch control at Op10 and Op12 while keeping the DES backbone intact. Under a common-random-number paired evaluation against a dense grid of static comparators, PPO improves Garrido/Excel resilience (ReT) by +0.000426 (95\% CI [0.000389, 0.000463]) over the best static policy, with matching gains in service continuity, backlog recovery, and tail cycle-time metrics. A 5-seed action-space ablation shows the gain concentrates in downstream-dispatch access specifically, ruling out ``more actuators'' as the explanation. The advantage holds across current and increased Garrido-native risk levels at both evaluated horizons, while severe risk is mixed and defines a scope boundary. The result also survives when the policy's access to the simulator's privileged disruption-regime signal is removed; conversely, a non-learning policy given that same privileged signal cannot approach the learned policy's performance. Retraining the original Track A action family on the same stress regime that favors Track B also fails to win, indicating the result depends on action-space coverage of the operational bottleneck rather than on the disruption regime alone. The contribution is not architectural novelty but a benchmark-centered, adversarially-tested diagnosis of when reinforcement learning adds resilience value in supply chain control, and when it does not.
\end{abstract}
