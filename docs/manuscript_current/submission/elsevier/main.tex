\title{When Does Reinforcement Learning Improve Supply Chain Resilience? Action-Space Alignment in a Garrido-Grounded DES Benchmark}
\begin{abstract}
Supply chain resilience studies increasingly combine simulation and reinforcement learning, yet it remains unclear when neural control improves resilience beyond strong static baselines. We build a Garrido-grounded discrete-event simulation benchmark of a 13-operation military food supply chain and evaluate PPO under common random numbers, dense static dispatch frontiers, and workbook-faithful Garrido/Excel resilience metrics. Under Track A, which preserves the original buffer/shift decision family, learned policies fail to convert the small measured headroom over the best static policy. Under Track B, a DES-preserving extension that adds downstream dispatch control at Op10 and Op12, PPO improves Excel ReT over the best dense downstream-dispatch static comparator by +0.000438 across 10 training seeds, with fully positive seed-clustered intervals and matching gains in flow fill, service-loss AUC, backlog, and recovery tails. The gain is not explained by privileged simulator fields: masked PPO retains most of the advantage, while a regime-conditioned non-learning lookup table remains far below PPO. An identified common-comparator factorial decomposes the gain: most of it comes from closed-loop control of the upstream and shift levers that the static family holds fixed, and adding downstream dispatch authority contributes a further, causally identified increment (joint minus no-dispatch $+0.000092$, CI95 excluding zero, all seeds positive); dispatch authority alone does not beat the dense frontier that already optimizes that subspace. This pattern operationalizes Theory-of-Constraints reasoning for RL action-space design in identified form: adaptive value concentrates on the levers that strong fixed policies hold constant, and reaching the binding constraint adds a further identified increment --- not more actuators or better rewards alone. A companion causal-prevention audit --- pre-event action interventions, exogenous risk-tape replay, and a perfect-foreknowledge ceiling test, cross-checked against adversarial controls that exposed and retracted an initial false-positive gate --- finds no evidence that the learned policy anticipates disruption before it occurs, across the full mediable risk roster; the measured advantage is adaptive recovery, not anticipatory preparation. We conclude that, in this benchmark, learned control earns its advantage by adaptively moving the levers that strong fixed policies hold constant, and earns more where the controllable interface also reaches the binding operational bottleneck --- with the headroom converted into adaptive recovery performance, not anticipation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Comparator-selection robustness}\label{appendix-robustness}

Because the headline comparator (the strongest of 147 statics) is
selected on the same evaluation episodes used to score PPO, comparator
selection could in principle inflate the reported delta (a
winner's-curse concern). Table~\ref{tab:top12_robustness} recomputes the
canonical paired delta against each of the twelve strongest static
comparators individually. All twelve deltas are positive with confidence
intervals excluding zero, and the delta \emph{grows} down the ranking ---
the opposite of the pattern selection bias would produce. Comparator
selection cannot flip the sign of the result.

\section{Efficiency-architecture attribution}\label{appendix-efficiency}

The prevention audit (Section~4.8) evaluated, among other candidates, a
policy trained with a live auxiliary future-risk prediction loss sharing
the feature trunk with the actor-critic. Its preventive claim did not
survive the negative controls, but a secondary observation did: under
the downstream-stress evaluation, that policy matched the reactive
baseline's resilience ($+10.05\%$ versus $+10.65\%$ over the best
static) at roughly 55\% of its resource cost (cost index 0.396 versus
0.719). A control ladder attributes the mechanism
(Figure~\ref{fig:efficiency_architecture}): replacing the true auxiliary
label with a temporally permuted label, a constant label, or removing
the auxiliary gradient entirely ($\lambda{=}0$, head present but inert)
all reproduce the same low-cost, equal-resilience cluster --- so neither
the predictive content nor the auxiliary loss is the cause. Conversely,
both default-extractor policies (the reactive baseline and the
clairvoyant variant with the true future visible) sit at $1.8$--$2.2\times$
the cost regardless of foreknowledge. The efficiency effect traces to
the alternative feature-extractor architecture itself (a narrow shared
trunk feeding separate actor and critic heads), not to prediction. We
report this as an incidental architecture observation --- potentially
useful where resource intensity matters --- and explicitly not as
evidence of anticipatory learning.
