\title{Action-Space Design in the Garrido-Grounded DES/RL Benchmark:\\
What We Changed, Why, and What It Produced}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\noindent This note documents, precisely and with full traceability to code
and data, the action-space decisions behind the two experimental lanes of
our reinforcement-learning benchmark on Garrido-Rios' (2017) military food
supply chain (MFSC) model. It answers three questions directly: (1) what
action contract did we actually use, and how does it relate to Garrido's
original discrete buffer/shift design; (2) why did the resulting learned
policies fail to beat the best static policy under the original decision
scope (Track A); and (3) what extension made a learned policy win, and why
do we believe that result. Every number cited here traces to a dated
artifact in the project repository; none are illustrative.
\end{abstract}

\section{Purpose}

Garrido-Rios' thesis studies two decision families for the MFSC: strategic
inventory buffers at three points in the chain (Op3, Op5, Op9) and
short-term manufacturing capacity via assembly shifts. Both were varied
\emph{discretely} and \emph{one factor at a time} in the original design of
experiments (DOE). Our starting proposal --- communicated directly to
Garrido --- was that this design-of-experiments grid could be replaced by a
much smaller, continuous parameterization: one continuous buffer variable
and one (effectively discrete, three-level) shift variable, instead of the
original 15 buffer configurations and 3 shift levels examined separately.
This note explains exactly what we built from that idea, what happened
when we trained a learned policy on it, why it did not win, and what we
changed afterward to obtain a genuine, robustly-tested win.

\section{Garrido-Rios' original decision variables}
\label{sec:garrido}

Table~\ref{tab:garrido_design} summarizes the thesis' design (Chapter 6,
Scenarios II--III). Three locations (Op3: raw-material warehouse, Op5:
pre-assembly buffer, Op9: ration supply battalion) can each hold a
strategic inventory buffer, replenished at one of five fixed intervals.
Separately, the assembly line can run at one of three shift levels. The
two families were never varied jointly: Scenario II fixes $S=1$ and varies
buffer levels (configurations Cf31--Cf60); Scenario III fixes buffer at
zero and varies shift level (Cf61--Cf90). This is a valid, efficient
one-factor-at-a-time DOE for a discrete-event simulation study, but it
means the thesis never directly observed what happens when buffer and
shift are adjusted together, and it never expressed either variable on a
continuous scale.

This gives $3 \times 5 = 15$ discrete buffer configurations and 3 discrete

configurations, Cf31--Cf90 in the thesis' numbering).

\section{From Garrido's grid to a trainable action space}
\label{sec:reduction}

A reinforcement-learning agent needs a differentiable, low-cardinality
action interface; training directly on Garrido's 15-cell buffer grid (or
worse, the full $15 \times 3 = 45$-cell joint grid he never populated)
would starve a policy-gradient method of usable signal between grid
points. The reduction we proposed to Garrido, and the further refinement
we made after testing it, proceeded in three steps (Figure~\ref{fig:chain}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_reduction_chain}
\caption{The action-space reduction chain actually implemented, from
Garrido's discrete design of experiments to the two experimental lanes of
the paper. Every stage keeps Garrido's own decision points; only the final
stage (Track B) adds a genuinely new lever.}
\label{fig:chain}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Step 1: \texttt{continuous\_its} --- the idea we proposed}

Our first implementation (\texttt{continuous\_its\_env.py},
contract \texttt{track\_a\_continuous\_its\_v1}) is exactly the
simplification described to Garrido: a single continuous scalar
$f \in [0,1]$, representing the fraction of the largest buffer level
($I_{1{,}344}$) applied \emph{commonly} to Op3, Op5, and Op9 simultaneously,
plus a single continuous shift signal mapped through fixed thresholds to
$S \in \{1,2,3\}$. This is a genuine two-dimensional action space: one
buffer dial, one shift dial. An early, small-scale screen (2 training
seeds) showed a promising adaptive signal on Excel ReT and CVaR, but a
3-seed confirmatory rerun and a subsequent dense common-random-number
(CRN) evaluation against a matching static frontier both failed to
reproduce it: the apparent win did not survive denser, fairer
static comparison. We record this honestly as an unconfirmed, superseded
exploratory lane, not as evidence for or against the central claim.

\subsection{Step 2: \texttt{per\_op\_buffer} --- the actual Track A
contract}

The single shared buffer fraction in Step 1 has a real limitation: it
forces the policy to raise or lower inventory at Op3, Op5, and Op9
together, even though Garrido's own design treats them as three
independent decision points. We removed this constraint
(\texttt{supply\_chain/continuous\_its\_env.py}, class
\texttt{PerOpBufferTrackAEnv}, contract
\texttt{track\_a\_per\_op\_buffer\_v1}): the action space becomes
\begin{equation}
a = (f_{\mathrm{Op3}},\, f_{\mathrm{Op5}},\, f_{\mathrm{Op9}},\, s) \in [0,1]^3 \times [-1,1],
\end{equation}
three independent continuous buffer-target fractions (each still expressed
relative to Garrido's own $I_{1{,}344}$ scale from Table~\ref{tab:garrido_design})
plus one continuous shift signal --- four dimensions in total. This is the
contract actually used for the paper's Track A headline result
(Section~\ref{sec:tracka}): it is strictly richer than the shared-fraction
version, and it preserves every one of Garrido's own decision points while
removing both his discretization into five fixed levels \emph{and} the
one-factor-at-a-time restriction (buffer and shift are now free to move
together). Table~\ref{tab:dof} compares the three designs directly.

\section{Track A: why the continuous relaxation still does not win}
\label{sec:tracka}

Before training any learner, we first asked whether the \emph{static}
decision space itself contains exploitable structure: a regime-conditioned
oracle (the best buffer/shift combination \emph{per disruption regime}) is
compared against the single best regime-agnostic constant policy over a
dense grid (five risk families $\times$ five frequency multipliers
$\times$ three impact multipliers). The oracle beats the best constant
policy by \textbf{+0.000176 to +0.000296 Excel ReT} (95\% CI lower bound
positive) --- a small but genuine, non-zero headroom. The question that
matters is whether a learned policy can capture it.

None of the tested learners do. We tried continuous PPO directly on the
\texttt{per\_op\_buffer} contract, PPO warm-started via behavior cloning on
the oracle's own action table, and PPO trained under a
family-conflict variant of the same contract with a
\texttt{ReT\_excel\_plus\_cvar} reward (5 seeds). The closest approach ---
behavior-cloning-warm-started PPO --- misses the best static comparator by
\[
-6.99\times10^{-6}\ \text{Excel ReT} \quad (0.155247\ \text{vs.}\ 0.155254),
\]
a gap two orders of magnitude smaller than the measured oracle headroom,
and is Pareto-dominated by ten static configurations on the joint
Excel-ReT/resource frontier (Table~\ref{tab:tracka}).

\subsection{The structural reason}

The reason is not that the buffer/shift decision space is poorly
parameterized. It is that the decision the agent controls is not the
constraint that governs recovery. Figure~\ref{fig:topology} marks the
binding bottleneck directly on the MFSC topology: downstream transport
throughput at Op10 and Op12 (line-of-communication dispatch to the combat
service support units and onward to theatre) runs at approximately 2,400--2,600
rations/day --- essentially equal to demand. Once a modest buffer is in
place, additional upstream inventory or additional assembly-line capacity
from a higher shift level cannot increase what actually reaches the
theatre, because the downstream pipe is already running near its ceiling.
Buffer and shift levers, however finely parameterized, sit entirely
upstream of this constraint. This is a direct instance of the
Theory-of-Constraints principle that system throughput is governed by its
binding constraint, not by average-case improvement elsewhere in the
chain --- and it explains why \emph{every} tested parameterization of the
buffer/shift family (discrete, shared-fraction continuous, and per-operation
continuous) converges to the same negative result.

\section{Track B: adding the one lever that reaches the bottleneck}
\label{sec:trackb}

Track B keeps every Track A decision variable and adds exactly the lever
that Section~\ref{sec:tracka} identifies as missing: transport dispatch
authority at Op10 and Op12. For this lane we also switched the upstream
parameterization from buffer \emph{fractions} to per-operation
\emph{multipliers} on order quantity and reorder point (a richer, six-dimensional
version of the same upstream family), so that the two new dispatch
dimensions could be added in the same multiplicative form
(Table~\ref{tab:action_contract}). The switch in upstream parameterization
is not what produces the result below --- Section~\ref{sec:tracka} already
showed that no parameterization of the upstream family alone wins --- it
is a modeling convenience for attaching the dispatch dimensions
consistently.

\subsection{Result}

Under the canonical confirmatory protocol (10 training seeds, 60{,}000
timesteps each, 12 CRN evaluation episodes per seed, paired against the
best of 147 dense static dispatch configurations), PPO improves Excel ReT
by
\[
+0.000438\ \text{(Excel ReT }0.005898\text{ vs.\ }0.005460\text{)},
\qquad \text{95\% CI } [+0.000409, +0.000468],
\]
with all ten per-seed mean deltas individually positive. The same policy
also produces much larger-magnitude gains in flow fill (0.961 vs.\ 0.669),
the recovery-tail cycle time $CT_j$ p99 (1{,}207\,h vs.\ 8{,}113\,h), and
service-loss AUC (an order-of-magnitude reduction). Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}
shows the policy relative to the entire static dispatch family: it is not
a strict cost win, but it dominates every one of the 147 static
configurations on the joint resilience/recovery-tail frontier.

\section{Why we believe this result}

The Track B win is not accepted on the strength of the headline number
alone. Four separate adversarial checks were run specifically to rule out
the most common alternative explanations for an RL win in a simulated
environment, and all four are consistent with the win being genuine and
attributable to the added dispatch authority specifically:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{More actuators, not more authority?} An action-space
ablation (5 seeds, matched scale) compares the full 8D contract against
downstream-dispatch-only (shift frozen) and shift-only (dispatch frozen)
variants. The downstream-only arm captures the \emph{largest} advantage
over its own best comparator ($+0.000429$), exceeding the full joint
contract ($+0.000367$); shift-only captures a comparable gain to joint
($+0.000377$). The gain does not scale with the number of controllable
dimensions --- it concentrates in access to the dispatch lever
specifically.
\item \textbf{Privileged simulator information?} We retrained PPO with the
seven observation fields that reveal the true disruption regime and its
forecast masked to zero; the masked policy retains approximately 95\% of
the canonical advantage ($+0.000403$/$+0.000395$ across two independent
compute environments, vs.\ $+0.000415$ unmasked). Separately, a
non-learning lookup table given \emph{direct} access to the true regime
(which no static comparator has) improves on the best constant policy by
only $+0.0000277$ --- a fraction of PPO's advantage over the same
comparator. The result does not depend on oracle information, in either
direction.
\item \textbf{Cost realism.} Because dispatch is the added lever, we
compute a dispatch-inclusive cost sensitivity
$C(\lambda_d) = C_{\mathrm{shift}} + \lambda_d[(m_{10}-1)_+ + (m_{12}-1)_+]$.
PPO uses lower average dispatch multipliers ($\approx$1.30/1.27) than the
best static comparator's constant 2.00/1.50, so once dispatch expediting
carries even a modest positive charge ($\lambda_d \geq 0.025$), PPO becomes
significantly cheaper in total, not just more resilient.
\item \textbf{Generalization.} The frozen canonical policy transfers with
fully positive, seed-clustered confidence intervals to current- and
increased-risk regimes at both evaluated horizons, re-validated against
fully re-optimized per-cell dense static frontiers (not just the training
regime); severe-risk, service-floor regimes are the disclosed boundary
case where the policy does not transfer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion: what we now believe, and why}

The evidence supports a single, structural explanation that is consistent
across every parameterization of the upstream decision family we tried ---
Garrido's own discrete design, our shared-fraction continuous relaxation,
and our richer per-operation continuous relaxation: \textbf{a learned
policy cannot convert measurable oracle headroom into a real win as long
as its action space is confined to decisions upstream of the operational
bottleneck.} The bottleneck in this MFSC is downstream transport dispatch
at Op10/Op12, whose capacity is calibrated in the thesis itself to sit
approximately at demand. Track A's negative result is therefore not an
artifact of discretization, of action-space dimensionality, or of any
single learner's tuning --- it survives across three genuinely different
parameterizations of the same decision family. Track B's positive result
is not an artifact of adding more actuators, of privileged observation, of
an unpriced free lever, or of overfitting to one stress regime --- it
survives four independent, purpose-built adversarial tests. Taken
together, we believe the central, defensible claim of this benchmark is:
reinforcement learning improves supply-chain resilience in this model when,
and only when, the controllable action surface reaches the constraint that
actually governs recovery.
